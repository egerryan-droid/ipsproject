# Strategic Groups — Integrated Protection Services (IPS)

**Capstone Strategic Analysis | March 2026**
**Team: Choi, Eger, McKean, Peat, Tucker**

---

## Executive Summary

The U.S. commercial fire protection and security integration industry is organizing into four distinct strategic groups defined by two primary dimensions: service breadth (narrow/specialized to full fire + security + XaaS) and geographic scope (regional to national). IPS occupies the lower-right quadrant of this map — a full-service regional integrator with $55 million in 2025 revenue, four metro markets (Cincinnati, Columbus, Lexington, Nashville), and a national accounts division that has operated in up to 48 states.

This position is the most defensible available to an independent integrator. It combines deep technical capability (NICET Level IV, combined fire + security, 25+ technology partnerships) with deep local relationships (25+ year customer tenure, AHJ trust, fastest response times). However, the position is under increasing pressure from two directions simultaneously: PE-backed national platforms are expanding downward into regional markets through acquisition, and cloud-native disruptors are expanding inward from electronic security toward full-service offerings. Every IPS market — Cincinnati, Columbus, Lexington, and Nashville — now has at least one PE-backed competitor present, with Cincinnati being the most contested.

A third dimension of competitive pressure emerges from the converged physical security analysis: **the profit in this industry is not in geographic reach or technical capability alone — it is in the migration from transactional project delivery to recurring managed services**. Companies that have made this migration (Convergint, Johnson Controls) command the highest margins, stickiest customer relationships, and strongest enterprise value multiples. IPS currently sits in the "advanced-but-transactional" quadrant — high capability delivered through a project-driven revenue model. The strategic imperative is not just to grow, but to migrate the business model rightward and upward: from project-driven to recurring, from hardware installation to intelligent managed platforms.

The central strategic implication is that IPS must grow aggressively enough to fund a partner buyout (42% silent partner, age 70) without selling to PE, while the mobility barriers that protect its position are being systematically eroded by well-capitalized competitors entering its markets. IPS's three-year revenue target of $82 million (2028) and acceleration of recurring revenue from 7-10% toward industry benchmarks are the most critical strategic priorities revealed by this analysis.

*Sources: IPS leadership meeting (March 20, 2026); IPS VTO (printed March 20, 2026); PitchBook [102-112]; Strategic groups — Converged Physical Security analysis (2026); industry competitor research (OH_KY_IN data)*

## 1. Strategic Group Definitions

The industry in IPS's markets organizes into four strategic groups. These groupings are determined by the two dimensions that most sharply differentiate competitive behavior: (1) the breadth of services offered, ranging from narrow electronic security to full fire + security + managed services, and (2) the geographic scope of operations, ranging from a single-metro focus to national or global presence.

---

### Group 1: PE-Backed National Platforms

**Defining Characteristics:** Very large scale (1,000+ employees), private equity ownership, aggressive M&A-driven growth, full-service fire and security capability, national or global geographic reach.

| Company | PE Owner | Revenue | Employees | IPS Market Overlap |
|---------|----------|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Convergint | Leonard Green (maj) / Ares (maj) / Harvest (min) | $2.7B (FY2024) | 11,000 | Cincinnati (Digital Visions, 2025), Nashville (Fiber Solutions, 2025) |
| Pye-Barker | Leonard Green (maj) / ADIA / GIC / Altas | $1.0B (FY2023); $6.1B valuation | 8,000 | Mason OH (Shiver Security), Somerset KY (Modern Systems), Louisville KY (Care Security) |
| Everon | GTCR | $781.7M (FY2024); $1.61B LBO valuation | 5,000 | Columbus OH, Louisville KY |
| Pavion | Wind Point Partners | $625.3M (FY2024, parent) | ~2,000+ (parent est.) | Cincinnati (via Turnkey Technology sister company) |
| Sciens | Carlyle Group | N/A ($390M raised) | 1,200 | Not yet in OH/KY/TN — expanding eastward |

**Strategic Behavior:** Growth through acquisition (Pye-Barker completed 57 acquisitions in 2025 alone). Centralized back-office operations with decentralized field execution. EBITDA margin targeting driven by PE exit timelines (typically 5-7 year hold periods). Cross-selling across acquired customer bases. Leveraging national scale to win enterprise and multi-location accounts. Coordinated market entry — Leonard Green holds majority stakes in both Convergint and Pye-Barker, controlling approximately $3.7 billion in combined fire and security integration revenue.

**Strengths:**
- Capital access: Pye-Barker has $680 million in undrawn credit capacity; Convergint has $2.70 billion in total debt facilities
- Scale advantages in purchasing, insurance, and enterprise sales
- Geographic reach for national and multi-location customers
- Acquisition capacity to enter any market within months
- Sovereign wealth fund validation (ADIA and GIC invested in Pye-Barker at $6.1B valuation, January 2025)

**Weaknesses:**
- Post-acquisition integration disruption — at 57 deals per year, cultural and operational integration is inevitably uneven
- Service quality inconsistency across acquired branches with different legacy cultures
- Local relationship erosion as decision-making centralizes to corporate headquarters
- High corporate overhead allocated to field operations
- PE timeline pressure creates short-term thinking — 5-7 year exit cycles incentivize margin extraction over long-term relationship investment
- Technician retention risk as acquired employees encounter new management, compensation structures, and corporate processes

### Group 2: Regional Independents (Full-Service)

**Defining Characteristics:** Mid-size (50-450+ employees), independently or family-owned, multi-service fire and security capability, strong local and regional presence, reputation-driven organic growth, conservative balance sheets.

| Company | Ownership | Employees | Revenue | Geography |
|---------|-----------|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **IPS** | Independent (2000) | ~140 W-2 (budgeting 152 EOY 2026) | **$55M (2025); $64M budget (2026)** | Cincinnati, Columbus, Lexington, Nashville + National Accounts |
| Silco Fire & Security | Independent (1931) | 450 | ~$60-80M est. | Cincinnati (HQ), Dayton, Akron (subsidiary) |

**Strategic Behavior:** Compete on service quality, technical expertise, and long-standing local relationships. Growth is predominantly organic, supplemented by reputation and referral networks. Resist or carefully evaluate PE acquisition offers. Invest in certifications and technology partnerships as differentiators. Make local decisions quickly without corporate approval chains. Maintain strong AHJ (Authority Having Jurisdiction) relationships that take years to build.

**Strengths:**
- Customer loyalty built over decades (IPS has 25+ year relationships with Xavier University, UC, Premier Health, Fifth Third, Kroger)
- Service responsiveness — local decision-making enables faster response than corporate-approval competitors
- AHJ relationships that cannot be acquired or replicated quickly
- Cultural continuity and employee retention advantages over PE-owned firms experiencing constant change
- Debt-free or conservative balance sheets (IPS is cash-positive with zero or minimal debt)
- Combined fire + security capability under one roof — rare even among larger competitors

**Weaknesses:**
- Scale limitations in purchasing power, R&D investment, and marketing spend
- Resource constraints for formal training programs, sales infrastructure, and technology investment
- Geographic limitations (IPS has 4 offices vs. Convergint's 220+)
- Profile as acquisition targets — the $15M-$80M independent integrator is exactly what PE roll-ups seek
- Vulnerability to key-person risk and succession challenges

**IPS Differentiation Within Group 2:** IPS is closing the revenue gap with Silco (~$60-80M estimated) and is expected to reach parity within 1-2 years at its current 20% organic growth rate. IPS is differentiated from Silco in several critical dimensions: NICET Level IV in-house certification, multi-state presence across four states (Silco has no Tennessee or Kentucky offices), national accounts capability with proven execution in up to 48 states (Amazon relationship), XaaS/cloud partnerships with Brivo and Eagle Eye Networks, status as the largest privately-held EST/Edwards distributor in the country (~$6M annual volume), and a broader technology partner lineup spanning 25+ vendor partnerships across fire, security, and managed services. Silco has deeper Ohio density with five offices versus IPS's two, and carries the weight of a 95-year operating history. IPS's $64M 2026 budget and 20% growth rate represent the fastest trajectory of any firm in this group.

### Group 3: Regional Independents (Niche/Expanding)

**Defining Characteristics:** Smaller scale (under 100 employees), independent ownership, may specialize in a subset of services (e.g., security-only, Pro AV adjacent), actively growing or pivoting into adjacent markets. Typically lack fire/life-safety capability or combined fire + security breadth.

| Company | Ownership | Employees | Geography |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Paladin Protective Systems | Independent (1990) | 60+ | Cleveland, Columbus; expanding to Cincinnati/Dayton |

**Strategic Behavior:** Geographic expansion into adjacent markets from a home-market base. Niche differentiation (Paladin specializes in Pro AV alongside security, creating a differentiated offering for certain commercial segments). Compete for mid-market customers where the largest nationals may be too expensive or too impersonal. May partner with other specialists to offer bundled solutions. Often serve as Brivo or cloud-platform channel partners alongside larger integrators.

**Strengths:**
- Agility and speed in decision-making
- Niche expertise that can win specific verticals or applications
- Lower overhead structures enabling competitive pricing
- Personal attention and owner involvement in customer relationships

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited scale constrains ability to serve large or multi-site customers
- Narrow service breadth — most lack fire/life-safety capability, suppression, or ERCES expertise
- Brand recognition limited to home market
- Vulnerable to being acquired by Group 1 platforms or outcompeted by Group 2 firms expanding into their territories
- Cannot match the certification depth (NICET IV) or manufacturer relationships of larger independents

*Sources: PitchBook research; IPS leadership meeting (March 20, 2026)*

### Group 4: Direct/Cloud-Native Disruptors

**Defining Characteristics:** Technology-first companies that bypass or supplement the traditional integrator channel. Sell cloud-managed solutions directly to IT departments or through hybrid channel models. Subscription-based recurring revenue. Focused exclusively on electronic security — cannot address fire alarm or life-safety requirements.

| Company | Model | Revenue | Focus |
|---------|-------|---------|-------|
| Verkada | Direct-to-customer + channel hybrid | $350M+ (2024); $4.5B valuation | Cloud video, access control, sensors |
| Brivo (merged with Eagle Eye) | Cloud platform for integrators | N/A (73,000+ locations) | ACaaS + VSaaS + AI platform |

**Strategic Behavior:** Sell cloud-managed solutions directly to IT departments or through channel partners, often bypassing traditional integrator relationships. Subscription-based revenue model with minimal on-site hardware and installation complexity. Rapid feature deployment through software updates. Compete on technology innovation, user experience, and total cost of ownership.

**Strengths:**
- Technology innovation and rapid product development cycles
- Built-in recurring revenue model (subscriptions from day one)
- Customer simplicity — single platform for multiple security functions
- Lower total cost of ownership for basic security applications
- Appeal to IT-centric buyers who prefer cloud-native solutions

**Weaknesses:**
- Cannot address fire alarm or life-safety requirements — these are code-mandated, require licensed installers, NICET-certified designers, and established AHJ relationships
- Verkada's closed ecosystem limits interoperability with existing systems and other vendors
- Limited to electronic security, unable to offer the full fire + security bundle that institutional and commercial customers increasingly demand
- Verkada has faced data breach and regulatory scrutiny, creating trust concerns for enterprise customers
- Distributor disintermediation creates channel conflict — Verkada "plays both sides" by selling direct while also working through integrators

**Dual Role of Brivo:** Brivo occupies a unique position as both IPS's most important complementor (cloud platform partner for ACaaS and VSaaS delivery) and a participant in this strategic group (sells through its own platform ecosystem to other integrators and end users). The January 2025 merger of Brivo and Eagle Eye Networks created a combined ACaaS + VSaaS + AI platform serving 73,000+ locations. For IPS, this partnership is the primary vehicle for XaaS/RMR growth — but it also creates platform dependency risk that IPS partially mitigates by maintaining alternative partnerships with Genetec, LenelS2, and Milestone.

*Sources: IPS leadership meeting (March 20, 2026); IPS Service Flyer (McHugh, 2024); PitchBook research*

## 2A. Converged Physical Security — Strategic Group Maps

The service breadth vs. geographic scope map (Section 2) captures the traditional competitive landscape. Two additional strategic group maps — developed from converged physical security analysis — reveal a more nuanced picture of where profit is actually generated, how competitors are positioning, and where IPS needs to migrate.

The critical insight: **growth is not geographic. Growth is moving right and up on both axes.** The companies generating the highest margins and stickiest revenue are not the ones with the most offices — they are the ones that have migrated from transactional hardware installation toward intelligent, recurring, managed security platforms. Every IPS strategic decision should be evaluated against this migration question: does this move us right and up?

**Four Quadrants Defined:**

- **Q1 (bottom-left): Basic Installers.** Transactional relationships, commodity hardware. Compete on price. Decision makers are facilities managers and procurement. Margins are lowest here. This is where Midwest Security, Royce, HSI, Executive Security, and Sec-Tron operate. IPS should be moving away from competing at this level — every bid IPS submits against commodity installers is a bid that compresses margins and distracts from higher-value work.

- **Q2 (top-left): Guarding Companies.** Recurring revenue through guard contracts and monitoring, but limited technology sophistication. Securitas, Allied Universal, Prosegur. Limited overlap with IPS today. The threat is through the bundling argument — "why use two vendors when we can do guards plus technology?" Securitas is actively pivoting toward a technology/data-led model, investing in remote monitoring and analytics to reduce guard headcount.

- **Q3 (bottom-right): Advanced-but-Transactional Integrators.** Strong technical capability — engineering depth, multi-vendor, multi-discipline — but revenue is project-driven. This is where IPS sits today, along with Silco and most regional integrators. The risk: high capability that gets commoditized because it is delivered transactionally. Customers rebid every project. No lock-in. IPS's 7-10% RMR confirms this positioning.

- **Q4 (top-right): Intelligent Managed Security.** Recurring revenue from managed services, SLA wraps, cyber hygiene for physical security, and lifecycle agreements — delivered through sophisticated integrated platforms. This is where Convergint deliberately migrated through managed services, strategic acquisitions, and SLA wrap. Johnson Controls has its proprietary platform, 250 cybersecurity engineers, and a national SOC. Everon is actively building managed service capabilities post-LBO. **This is where profit is maximized and where IPS needs to be.**

**The Migration Question:** IPS's strategic priority is to migrate from Q3 to Q4 — from advanced-but-transactional to intelligent-managed. This requires three shifts: (1) pre-design advisory work to own the specification before the bid, (2) lifecycle agreements with cyber hygiene for physical security systems, and (3) recurring revenue across the full multi-discipline scope (fire, access, video, intrusion, suppression). Geographic expansion alone does not achieve this migration. Adding offices without changing the revenue model just creates a bigger Q3 company.

### Movement 1: PE-Backed Nationals Expanding Downward (Group 1 into Group 2 Territory)

PE-backed platforms are systematically entering regional markets through acquisition. This is not speculative — it is documented and accelerating. Convergint entered Cincinnati (Digital Visions acquisition) and Nashville (Fiber Solutions) in 2025. Pye-Barker acquired three companies in Ohio and Kentucky (Shiver Security in Mason OH, Modern Systems in Somerset KY, Care Security in Louisville KY). Everon established offices in Columbus and Louisville. With $680 million in undrawn capacity at Pye-Barker alone, and 57 acquisitions completed in a single year, the pace of downward expansion will continue through 2028. The projected outcome is that by 2028, every metro market with $100M+ in fire and security spending will have at least two PE-backed competitors present, and markets like Cincinnati will have five or more.

### Movement 2: Cloud-Native Disruptors Expanding Service Breadth (Group 4 Rightward)

Cloud-native companies are expanding from narrow electronic security into broader managed-service offerings. The Brivo-Eagle Eye merger (January 2025) created a combined ACaaS + VSaaS + AI analytics platform. Verkada has added access control, environmental sensors, and intercom to its video-centric platform. By 2028, Group 4 players will offer near-complete electronic security suites through cloud delivery — closing the gap with traditional integrators on the security side. However, they will remain unable to address fire/life-safety (code-mandated, requires licensed installers and NICET certification), preserving the moat for full-service integrators like IPS. The key implication is that fire alarm capability becomes an even more critical differentiator as cloud platforms commoditize basic electronic security.

### Movement 3: IPS Growth Trajectory (Group 2, Expanding Scale and RMR)

IPS's own trajectory within Group 2 is aggressive. The three-year EOS target calls for growth from $55M (2025) to $82M (2028), representing a 49% increase over three years. Headcount is projected to grow from 140 to 180 employees, and the branch footprint from 4 to 6 offices (with Atlanta as the most likely next market, followed by a sixth location to be determined). Target margins improve from 30% GM / 10% EBITDA (2026) to 33% GM / 12% EBITDA (2028). Most critically, IPS must shift its recurring revenue percentage from the current 7-10% toward a level that significantly increases enterprise value multiples — enabling the partner buyout without PE involvement. If IPS executes on this trajectory, it will be the largest independent fire and security integrator in the Ohio-Kentucky-Tennessee corridor by 2028, with a geographic footprint and revenue base that creates meaningful barriers to acquisition-driven displacement.

**On the converged physical security maps, IPS must simultaneously move from Q3 toward Q4** — migrating from advanced-but-transactional to intelligent-managed. This means the $82M revenue target is necessary but not sufficient. IPS must also shift its revenue mix: pre-design advisory engagements, lifecycle agreements with embedded cyber hygiene, and multi-discipline managed services. Without this Q3-to-Q4 migration, IPS reaches $82M as a larger version of what it is today — still project-driven, still vulnerable to commoditization, still scoring 2.25 on service complexity while Convergint scores 2.88.

### Group 3: Consolidation and Attrition

Group 3 (Regional Niche) is projected to shrink by 2028. Smaller independents under $15M in revenue are the primary acquisition targets for PE roll-up platforms — they are cheaper to acquire, easier to integrate, and often lack succession plans. Paladin's expansion into Cincinnati/Dayton puts it directly in competition with both IPS and Silco, and in the sights of PE acquirers looking for Cincinnati-area footholds. The projected outcome is fewer independent niche players and more PE-owned regional branches by 2028.

## 4. Mobility Barriers Analysis

Mobility barriers are the structural factors that prevent firms from easily moving between strategic groups. These barriers define the stability of the group structure and the defensibility of each group's competitive position.

### Barriers to Entry into Group 1 (PE-Backed National Platforms)

| Barrier | Height | Explanation |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Capital requirements | Very High | Requires hundreds of millions to billions in PE capital. Pye-Barker's $6.1B valuation and $4.31B debt structure are representative. No organic path exists. |
| Acquisition integration capability | High | Managing 50+ acquisitions per year requires sophisticated integration playbooks, centralized finance/HR systems, and experienced M&A teams. |
| National customer relationships | High | Enterprise accounts (Fortune 500, multi-site retailers) require proven national delivery capability, insurance/bonding capacity, and standardized SLAs across geographies. |
| Debt capacity and financial engineering | Very High | First-lien term loans of $700M-$2.4B, delayed-draw term loans, revolvers — requires institutional lender relationships and PE sponsor credibility. |

**Implication for IPS:** IPS cannot organically enter Group 1. The only path would be accepting PE investment, which leadership explicitly wants to avoid. This is a strategic choice, not a constraint — IPS is choosing to compete from Group 2.

### Barriers to Entry into Group 2 (Regional Full-Service)

| Barrier | Height | Explanation |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Dual licensing (fire + security) | High | Requires separate contractor licenses for fire alarm and electronic security in each state. Ohio, Kentucky, and Tennessee each have distinct licensing requirements. |
| NICET Level IV certification | Very High | Requires 10+ years of progressive experience and rigorous examination. Cannot be acquired or accelerated. IPS has in-house NICET IV engineers — most competitors do not. |
| Manufacturer relationships | High | IPS's position as the largest privately-held EST/Edwards distributor (~$6M annual volume) took 25+ years to build. Edwards protected regions in some markets further limit competitor access. |
| AHJ relationships | High | Relationships with local Authorities Having Jurisdiction (fire marshals, building inspectors) are built through years of code-compliant installations and responsive service. Trust is personal and non-transferable. |
| Installed base lock-in | Moderate-High | IPS's existing customer installations create switching costs — fire alarm systems are proprietary (Edwards panels require Edwards-trained technicians), inspection contracts renew annually, and replacement is expensive and disruptive. |
| Local reputation and references | Moderate-High | 25+ year track record with anchor institutions (Xavier University, Premier Health, Fifth Third, Kroger) provides reference depth that new entrants cannot match. |

**Implication for IPS:** These barriers are IPS's primary defensive moat. They explain why PE-backed nationals enter regional markets through acquisition rather than organic buildout — it is faster to buy an existing integrator with licenses, relationships, and an installed base than to replicate them from scratch.

### Barriers to Movement from Group 4 into Group 2 (Cloud-Native to Full-Service)

| Barrier | Height | Explanation |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Fire/life-safety licensing | Very High | Fire alarm installation and inspection require state-specific contractor licenses, NICET-certified designers, and compliance with NFPA 72 and local fire codes. Cloud companies have no path to this capability without acquiring licensed firms. |
| NICET certification pipeline | Very High | Cannot be replicated through technology — requires individual technicians with 10+ years of hands-on experience. |
| AHJ relationships | High | Fire marshals and building inspectors work with known, trusted contractors. A cloud platform vendor has no existing relationships in this ecosystem. |
| Physical installation workforce | High | Fire alarm and suppression systems require skilled field technicians for installation, commissioning, and ongoing inspection. Cloud companies have minimal field workforce infrastructure. |
| Code compliance expertise | High | NFPA 72 (fire alarm), NFPA 13 (sprinkler), NFPA 25 (inspection/testing), IFC, and local amendments create a complex regulatory environment that requires deep institutional knowledge. |

**Implication for IPS:** The fire/life-safety barrier is the single most important structural advantage in IPS's competitive position. Cloud-native disruptors can commoditize basic electronic security (video, access control) but cannot touch fire alarm, suppression, ERCES, or mass notification. IPS's fire-alarm-first bundling strategy — using fire as the anchor and layering security, access control, video, managed services, and new categories on top — is structurally sound because it leverages the highest mobility barrier in the industry.

### Barriers to Movement from Group 3 into Group 2 (Niche to Full-Service)

| Barrier | Height | Explanation |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Service breadth expansion | Moderate-High | Adding fire alarm capability to a security-only firm requires separate licensing, hiring NICET-certified engineers, establishing manufacturer relationships (Edwards, Fike), and building AHJ trust. |
| Geographic expansion capital | Moderate | Opening new offices requires upfront investment in leases, equipment, local hires, and marketing before revenue materializes. IPS's Nashville experience shows that hiring the right local leader is critical. |
| Manufacturer distributorship agreements | High | Protected distribution regions and volume commitments create barriers. IPS's Edwards distributorship took decades to build. |

**Implication for IPS:** Group 3 firms expanding into IPS markets (e.g., Paladin entering Cincinnati/Dayton) are a competitive nuisance but not an existential threat. They lack fire capability, NICET depth, and the manufacturer relationships needed to compete for the institutional and commercial accounts that drive IPS's revenue.

### Barriers to Q3-to-Q4 Migration (Advanced-Transactional to Intelligent-Managed)

The converged physical security analysis identifies a fifth mobility barrier set that is uniquely relevant to IPS's strategic trajectory:

| Barrier | Height | Explanation |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Sales capability transformation | High | Selling managed services to CSOs/CISOs requires different skills, relationships, and value propositions than selling project work to facilities managers. IPS's current sales team is not trained for this conversation. |
| Recurring revenue infrastructure | Moderate-High | Managed service delivery requires NOC/SOC capability, SLA frameworks, cyber hygiene protocols for physical security, and lifecycle management systems — none of which IPS currently operates at scale. |
| Customer education | Moderate | Institutional customers accustomed to transactional project procurement must be educated on the value of lifecycle agreements and managed security. This is a market-making exercise, not a bid-board exercise. |
| Pricing model transformation | Moderate | Shifting from project-based pricing (lump sum, competitive bid) to subscription/managed pricing (monthly recurring, value-based) requires financial modeling capability, customer ROI tools, and sales incentive realignment. |

**Implication for IPS:** The Q3-to-Q4 migration is the strategic priority that underpins every other goal. It is harder than geographic expansion (which is operational) because it requires changing how IPS sells, what IPS delivers, and who IPS sells to. But it is also the migration that generates the recurring revenue needed to fund the partner buyout and the enterprise value multiples needed to make that buyout financially viable.

## 5. PE Encirclement Analysis

IPS's core markets are being systematically entered by PE-backed competitors through acquisition. This encirclement has intensified dramatically in 2024-2025 and represents the single most important competitive development in IPS's operating environment.

### Market-by-Market Encirclement

|------------|------------------------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Cincinnati** | Convergint, Pavion, Pye-Barker, Silco (independent) | Convergint acquired Digital Visions (Jun 2025); Pavion sister company Turnkey Technology; Pye-Barker acquired Shiver Security in Mason, OH | **Very High** — 4 PE-backed + 1 large independent + IPS |

**Key Finding: Every IPS market now has at least one PE-backed competitor present.** This is a structural shift from even two years ago, when most of IPS's markets were contested primarily among regional independents. The encirclement pattern is not random — PE platforms are deliberately targeting the same mid-size metro markets where IPS operates because these markets have attractive commercial construction activity, institutional healthcare and education customers, and fragmented competitive landscapes ripe for roll-up.

IPS's debt-free balance sheet and $55M revenue are a strength (financial discipline, no PE timeline pressure) but also a vulnerability — the company cannot match the acquisition budgets, marketing spend, or below-cost bidding that PE-backed competitors can deploy in pursuit of market share.

### Leonard Green Dual-Platform Control

The most strategically significant finding in the PE landscape is that Leonard Green & Partners holds majority stakes in both Convergint ($2.7B revenue) and Pye-Barker ($1.0B revenue). A single PE firm controls approximately $3.7 billion in combined fire and security integration revenue and 19,000 employees. Both platforms are now present in Cincinnati — IPS's home market. This creates the potential for coordinated market strategy within antitrust limits, combined purchasing leverage with manufacturers like Edwards/Kidde, and a shared acquisition playbook that has already identified the Ohio-Kentucky-Tennessee corridor as a target-rich environment.

### Sovereign Wealth Fund Validation

The January 2025 Pye-Barker recapitalization at $6.1 billion brought Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA) and GIC (Singapore's sovereign wealth fund) into the fire/security sector. This signals that the world's most sophisticated institutional investors view fire and security services as a durable, inflation-protected asset class with long-term compounding potential. The strategic implication is that capital flows into fire/security consolidation will not slow down — they will intensify as more institutional investors seek exposure to this validated asset class.

### Encirclement Implications for IPS

1. **Talent competition intensifies.** PE-backed acquirers entering IPS markets bring signing bonuses, equity participation, and career-path narratives that IPS must counter with culture, stability, and local ownership advantages.

2. **Pricing pressure on large projects.** PE platforms can absorb losses on initial projects to establish market presence, knowing they will make it back through cross-selling and long-term contracts. IPS cannot engage in below-cost bidding.

3. **Acquisition of IPS's potential M&A targets.** If IPS identifies a small fire alarm company in Atlanta or another expansion market, PE platforms with $680M in undrawn capacity can outbid IPS on price and speed of close.

4. **Customer uncertainty.** Large institutional customers may question whether IPS has the scale to compete long-term, creating an opening for PE-backed competitors to position themselves as the "safer" choice despite their inferior local relationships.

5. **Manufacturer relationship pressure.** Edwards/Kidde (now under Lone Star Funds PE ownership) is adding distributor partners in IPS territories, weakening the exclusivity that has been a core competitive advantage for over two decades.

## 6. Strategic Implications for IPS

### Defending the Lower-Right Quadrant — And Migrating Toward Q4

IPS's position — full-service, regional focus — is defensible but not invulnerable. The strategic imperative is twofold: (1) deepen the moat around the current position while growing fast enough to make acquisition economically unattractive to PE buyers and operationally viable for funding the partner buyout, and (2) migrate the business model from advanced-but-transactional (Q3 on the converged physical security map) to intelligent-managed (Q4) — because that is where margins, retention, and enterprise value multiples are maximized.

**Priority 1: Accelerate RMR as the enterprise-value multiplier.** IPS's true recurring revenue of 7-10% is the single largest gap between current state and the financial profile needed to fund the partner buyout. Service margins (~50%) are 2.5x project margins (~20%). Every percentage point of revenue shifted from project to recurring directly increases the enterprise value multiple available for financing the 42% buyout. The Brivo/Eagle Eye platform partnership and cloud migration trend provide the vehicle; the gap is in go-to-market execution and sales incentive alignment.

**Priority 2: Execute the Q3-to-Q4 migration through three mechanisms.** (1) Pre-design advisory work — own the specification before the bid goes out. When IPS writes the spec, IPS wins the bid. This shifts the competitive dynamic from price-based bidding to relationship-based advisory. (2) Lifecycle agreements with cyber hygiene for physical security systems — package firmware updates, vulnerability assessments, and device lifecycle management into recurring contracts. This is what Convergint does. This is what Johnson Controls does with 250 cybersecurity engineers and a national SOC. IPS can do it locally, with the speed and personal accountability that nationals cannot match. (3) Recurring revenue across the full multi-discipline scope — fire inspection, monitoring, access control managed services, video analytics, and specialty systems (ERCES, weapons detection, nurse call) all bundled into a single managed relationship. The bundle is the moat.

**Priority 3: Leverage the fire-alarm-first bundling strategy.** The mobility barrier analysis confirms that fire/life-safety is the most durable competitive advantage in the industry — it cannot be replicated by cloud disruptors and takes years for PE-backed acquirers to build organically. IPS should position every customer engagement with fire alarm as the anchor, layering security, access control, video, managed services, and specialty categories (ERCES, weapons detection, nurse call) on top. This bundle is the strongest anti-substitution and anti-displacement strategy available.

**Priority 4: Execute geographic expansion ahead of PE encirclement.** Atlanta is the next market. IPS should replicate the Nashville model (hire a local industry leader, establish AHJ relationships, pursue small fire alarm acquisitions to gain an installed base). The window for entering new markets as an independent is closing as PE platforms acquire available targets. Each market IPS enters first becomes harder for PE to displace through acquisition.

**Priority 5: Convert the installed base before competitors do.** IPS has a 25+ year installed base of fire alarm and security customers across four metro markets. Every customer running on-premise, legacy systems is a conversion opportunity for cloud-managed services — and also a target for PE-backed competitors offering "free" technology upgrades as part of acquisition-driven customer migration. IPS must proactively engage its installed base with XaaS migration offers before competitors reach them. The converged physical security analysis confirms that Everon and Johnson Controls are particularly dangerous here — they price competitively on the install, then lock clients into managed service contracts. IPS must get there first.

**Priority 6: Build workforce capacity as the binding growth constraint.** The two-year technician ramp, absence of a formal apprenticeship program, and acknowledged gaps in sales training are binding constraints on growth. IPS cannot grow to $82M by 2028 with 140 employees. The EOS target of 180 employees requires hiring approximately 50 people (net of attrition) across four markets over three years — including NICET-tracked technicians who take years to certify. Workforce development is a strategic priority, not an HR function.

**Priority 7: Maintain cultural differentiation as the anti-PE moat.** IPS leadership's 25-year track record of competitive wins against larger players is rooted in a culture of responsiveness, technical expertise, and personal accountability that PE-owned firms structurally struggle to replicate. Every post-acquisition integration disruption at a competitor is an opportunity for IPS to win displaced customers and recruit displaced technicians. This advantage compounds over time — but only if IPS actively invests in the culture (IPS University, core values reinforcement, branch autonomy within EOS discipline) that makes it possible.

## 7. Conclusion

The strategic group analysis reveals an industry in structural transition. PE capital is compressing the map — national platforms are moving downward into regional markets while cloud disruptors are moving rightward toward broader service offerings. The group structure that existed five years ago, when regional independents competed primarily against each other, is being replaced by one where every market is a PE battleground.

The converged physical security analysis adds a critical third dimension to this picture: **the migration from transactional to recurring is where enterprise value is created.** The traditional strategic group map (service breadth vs. geographic scope) explains who competes where. The physical security quadrant model (solution sophistication vs. customer relationship depth) explains who makes money. IPS is well-positioned on the first map. It is underperforming on the second. Closing that gap — moving from Q3 to Q4, from 7-10% RMR to 15%+, from project-driven to lifecycle-managed — is the defining strategic priority.

Three threats converge on IPS simultaneously: commoditization from below (basic installers compressing margins on lower-end bids), displacement from above (PE-backed nationals entering IPS markets with capital, scale, and managed service platforms), and convergence from the side (IT/cyber managed service providers like Vitis adding physical security to their portfolios). The recommended hybrid strategy — pre-design advisory, lifecycle agreements with cyber hygiene, recurring revenue across full multi-discipline scope — addresses all three threat vectors simultaneously by positioning IPS in the quadrant where none of these threats can easily follow.

IPS occupies the most defensible position available to an independent integrator. The combination of NICET IV certification, combined fire + security capability, the largest privately-held EST/Edwards distributorship nationally, 25+ year customer relationships, and independent ownership creates a moat that is genuinely difficult to replicate. But moats erode if not reinforced.

The partner buyout is the forcing function. IPS must grow from $55M to $82M, shift recurring revenue from 7-10% toward industry benchmarks, expand to 6 branches, and maintain 33% gross margins and 12% EBITDA — all within three years, all without PE capital, and all while PE-backed competitors with billions in deployed capital systematically enter every market IPS operates in. This is the defining strategic challenge revealed by the strategic group analysis: the intersection of PE consolidation pressure with an internal ownership transition that demands growth, independence, and financial discipline simultaneously. The converged physical security maps make the path clear: grow right and up. Not just bigger — smarter.
