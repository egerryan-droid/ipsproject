## Executive Summary

IPS operates in a $22+ billion U.S. fire protection and security installation industry undergoing rapid PE-driven consolidation. With $55 million in 2025 revenue, a $64 million 2026 budget (~20% organic growth), and a conservative debt-free balance sheet, IPS is one of the largest independently-held fire and security integrators in the country — and one of very few above $15M in revenue that have not sold to private equity. Every one of IPS's four markets — Cincinnati, Columbus, Lexington, and Nashville — now has at least one PE-backed national competitor present, with Cincinnati being the most contested.

IPS is positioned in the lower-right quadrant of the strategic group map: full-service with regional focus. This is the most defensible position for an independent integrator, combining deep service capability with deep local relationships. IPS's competitive advantages — NICET Level IV certification, combined fire-plus-security capability, the largest privately-held EST/Edwards distributorship nationally (~$6M/year), independent ownership, and 25+ year customer relationships — are durable but face growing pressure from well-capitalized national platforms.

The defining strategic reality is the partner buyout: a silent partner who owns 42% of IPS is age 70 and wants to exit. IPS leadership wants to fund this buyout without PE. Growth (especially RMR growth) is therefore existential — it directly drives the enterprise value multiples needed to finance the transaction. IPS's current true recurring revenue of 7-10% is well below industry benchmarks, making RMR acceleration the single highest-leverage strategic priority.

*Sources: IPS leadership meeting (March 20, 2026); IPS VTO (printed March 20, 2026); IPS Service Flyer (McHugh, 2024); PitchBook [102-112]*

The commercial segment represents over 47% of the fire protection market globally (~$39.49B in 2023). This is IPS's primary market. IPS operates at the intersection of two NAICS codes — security systems ($33.2B) and electrical contracting ($223.6B) — giving it a combined addressable market exceeding $250B. At $55M in revenue, IPS is 11.5x the average security systems firm ($4.79M) and represents less than 0.02% of the total addressable market, indicating significant room for growth.

### IPS Addressable Market

IPS operates at the intersection of commercial fire alarm and detection, commercial electronic security integration, and managed security services (ACaaS, VSaaS). With $55M in 2025 revenue (~50% fire alarm, ~50% security), IPS serves commercial, institutional, and industrial customers through four metro markets (Cincinnati/Dayton, Columbus, Lexington/Louisville, Nashville) plus a national accounts division that has operated in up to 48 states. The addressable market encompasses new construction, retrofit and upgrade, service and maintenance, and managed services — with the cloud/XaaS layer (16-18% CAGR) growing 3-4x faster than traditional integration revenue. IPS's current project-to-service ratio (75/25) and low RMR (7-10%) indicate significant upside in converting its installed base to recurring revenue models.

The industry in IPS's markets organizes into five strategic groups based on service breadth and geographic scope.

**Strengths:** Capital, scale, geographic reach, enterprise relationships, acquisition capacity.

**Weaknesses:** Post-acquisition integration disruption, service quality inconsistency, local relationship erosion, high corporate overhead, PE timeline pressure (5–7 year exit cycles create short-term thinking).

### Group 2: Regional Independents (Full-Service)

**Characteristics:** Mid-size (50–300+ employees), independently or family-owned, multi-service fire and security, strong local presence, reputation-driven growth.

| Company | Ownership | Employees | Revenue | Geography |
|---------|-----------|-----------|---------|-----------|

*Source: PitchBook [105]*

**Strategic behavior:** Compete on service quality, technical expertise, and local relationships. Organic growth supplemented by reputation. Resist or evaluate acquisition offers. Invest in certifications and technology partnerships.

**Strengths:** Customer loyalty, service responsiveness, AHJ relationships, fast local decision-making, cultural continuity.

**Weaknesses:** Scale limitations, resource constraints, geographic limitations, profile as acquisition targets.

**IPS within this group:** At $55M (2025 revenue) and ~140 employees, IPS is closing the gap with Silco (~$60-80M est., 450 employees). IPS is differentiated from Silco by NICET IV certification, multi-state presence (Silco has no TN or KY offices), national accounts capability (48-state reach via Amazon relationship), XaaS partnerships with Brivo, and status as the largest privately-held EST/Edwards distributor in the country. IPS also has a broader technology partner lineup (25+ vendor partnerships across fire, security, and managed services). Silco has deeper Ohio density with 5 offices vs. IPS's 2 Ohio offices. IPS's 20% organic growth rate and $64M 2026 budget suggest revenue parity with Silco within 1-2 years.

*Sources: IPS leadership meeting (March 20, 2026); PitchBook [105]*

### Group 3: Regional Independents (Niche/Expanding)

**Characteristics:** Smaller (under 100 employees), independent, may specialize in a subset of services, actively growing or pivoting.

**Strategic behavior:** Geographic expansion into adjacent markets; niche differentiation (Paladin specializes in Pro AV alongside security); compete for mid-market customers.

**Strengths:** Agility, niche expertise, lower overhead.

**Weaknesses:** Limited scale, narrow service breadth, brand recognition limited to home market.

### Group 4: Direct/Cloud-Native Disruptors

**Characteristics:** Technology-first companies that bypass or supplement the traditional integrator channel.

| Company | Model | Revenue | Focus |
|---------|-------|---------|-------|
| Verkada | Direct-to-customer + channel hybrid | $350M+ (2024) | Cloud video, access control, sensors |
| Brivo (merged with Eagle Eye) | Cloud platform for integrators | N/A (73,000+ locations) | ACaaS + VSaaS + AI platform |

**Strategic behavior:** Sell cloud-managed solutions directly to IT departments or through channel partners. Subscription-based revenue. Minimize on-site hardware and installation complexity.

**Strengths:** Technology innovation, recurring revenue model, customer simplicity, rapid feature deployment.

**Weaknesses:** Cannot address fire alarm or life-safety (code-mandated, requires licensed installers). Verkada's closed ecosystem limits interoperability. Limited to electronic security.

**Note:** Brivo occupies a dual role — it is both IPS's most important complementor (platform partner) and a participant in this strategic group (sells through its own platform ecosystem).

### Group 5: Converged Security/IT Providers

**Characteristics:** Large-scale companies converging physical security with cybersecurity, IT services, or guard services. Offer integrated solutions that span physical and digital domains.

| Company | Model | Revenue | IPS Market Overlap |
|---------|-------|---------|-------------------|
| Allied Universal | Guards + technology + cyber | $20B+ (largest private security globally) | OH/IN/KY offices |
| Securitas | Guards + remote monitoring + cyber | $15.3B (global) | Tech HQ Uniontown OH |
| Convergint (also Group 1) | Fire + physical + cyber (via Deloitte) | $2.7B | Cincinnati, Nashville |
| Vitis Technologies | IT/cyber + physical security | Small (local) | Blue Ash OH (Cincinnati metro) |
| Cintas Fire Protection | Fire protection/alarms/sprinklers/extinguishers | Part of $9.6B Cintas Corp (Fortune 500) | Cincinnati HQ — direct fire competitor |

**Strategic behavior:** Bundle cybersecurity assessments, network security, and managed IT with physical security deployments. Target enterprise customers who want a single vendor for all security needs. Guard companies (Allied, Securitas) are acquiring technology companies to add electronic security.

**Strengths:** Scale, enterprise relationships, ability to offer "one throat to choke" for all security needs, cyber expertise IPS lacks.

**Weaknesses:** Fire/life-safety is NOT their core — they lack NICET certification, Edwards/EST relationships, and AHJ trust. Guard-to-tech transitions are culturally difficult. Cintas is fire-focused but primarily suppression/extinguishers, not the alarm/detection systems that are IPS's core.

**Threat to IPS:** MODERATE but GROWING. The convergence of physical and cybersecurity is a real trend. IPS confirmed it has no client-facing cybersecurity capability (Pete Keller, March 2025). Convergint already offers cyber through its Deloitte partnership. As enterprise buyers increasingly demand integrated physical + cyber solutions, IPS's lack of cyber capability becomes a gap — not yet a crisis, but a vector competitors will exploit.

## 8. Conclusions and Strategic Positioning

### IPS's Defensible Position

IPS occupies a strategically sound position in the industry: full-service capability with deep regional roots, $55M in revenue, 20% organic growth, and a debt-free balance sheet. The fire and life-safety moat — built on NICET IV certification, multi-state licensing, AHJ relationships, the largest privately-held EST/Edwards distributorship in the country, and 25+ years of customer relationships with institutions like Xavier University, Premier Health, Fifth Third, and Kroger — is the foundation of this position. It cannot be replicated quickly by new entrants, cloud disruptors, or even PE-backed acquirers (who gain market presence through acquisition but often struggle to maintain the service quality and relationship depth that independent integrators provide).

IPS leadership confirmed this competitive confidence directly: after 25 years of competitive wins against larger players, they view competition as a catalyst rather than a threat. The real strategic challenge is internal — executing growth fast enough to fund the partner buyout while maintaining the culture and service quality that differentiates IPS.

### The Central Strategic Question

The defining competitive reality is no longer just PE consolidation — it is the intersection of PE consolidation with IPS's partner buyout timeline. A silent partner who owns 42% of the business is 70 years old and wants to exit. IPS leadership wants to fund this buyout without PE involvement. This creates a growth imperative that compounds the external competitive pressure.

IPS needs to simultaneously:

1. **Grow revenue** from $55M toward the $82M three-year target (2028) and $150M ten-year target (2034)
2. **Grow RMR** from 7-10% toward a level that significantly increases enterprise value multiples
3. **Maintain independence** — avoid the PE sale that most $15M+ integrators have already made
4. **Fund the buyout** — generate enough enterprise value to purchase 42% of the company from the retiring partner

### Priority Actions (Updated with Primary Data)

1. **Accelerate XaaS/RMR conversion** — This is the single highest-leverage strategic priority. IPS's true recurring revenue (7-10%) is far below what drives premium multiples. Every percentage point of RMR shift directly increases enterprise value for the partner buyout. The industry is moving toward subscription models (manufacturers mandating it), customer resistance to cloud has decreased significantly, and service margins (~50%) are 2.5x project margins (~20%). IPS already has the platform partnerships (Brivo, Eagle Eye, Genetec, LenelS2) — the gap is in go-to-market execution and sales incentive alignment.

2. **Shift from farming to hunting (market-making)** — IPS leadership identified this as a critical growth gap. Established branches (Cincinnati, Columbus) are working bid boards and have more opportunities than they can handle, but this is reactive. Sustaining 20% organic growth requires proactive market-making: identifying new customers, targeting underserved verticals, and penetrating facilities still running proprietary (Johnson Controls, Siemens) or banned (Hikvision, Dahua) equipment. This aligns with IPS's VTO goal of market-making initiatives.

3. **Launch Atlanta as next geographic expansion** — Atlanta is IPS leadership's most likely next market. Existing customers are already requesting service there, it's a growing Southeast market with strong construction activity, it's within reasonable proximity to Nashville for shared resources, and it has excellent airport access. The Nashville model (hire a local industry leader, not a transplant) should be replicated. For fire alarm, pursue targeted acquisitions of small fire alarm companies in the Atlanta metro to gain an installed base for immediate recurring revenue.

4. **Bring in-house services that currently leak to subcontractors** — IPS currently subs out fire extinguisher maintenance, emergency light repairs, and some sprinkler work. Bringing these in-house creates new recurring revenue streams, deepens customer relationships, and removes the inconsistency of third-party service quality. This is low-risk product category expansion that aligns with VTO goal of vetting product categories.

5. **Invest in workforce capacity and formalize training** — The 2-year technician ramp, absence of formal apprenticeship programs, and acknowledged gaps in sales training are binding constraints on growth. IPS should invest in an IPS University (on the VTO), formalize a technician development pathway with NICET certification milestones, and create a branch "launch team" playbook for new market entry. Leadership succession planning (also on the VTO) is critical for geographic expansion.

6. **Leverage the fire-alarm-first bundling strategy** — Fire alarm is the anchor: stickier (compliance-mandated), higher-barrier, and drives recurring inspection and monitoring revenue. Security, access control, video, managed services, and new categories (door hardware, weapons detection) layer on top. This bundling is IPS's most effective competitive and anti-substitution strategy against both PE-backed nationals (who may lack the local fire expertise) and cloud-native disruptors (who cannot address fire/life-safety at all).

## The Third Front: EC Vertical Integration

The competitive analysis above focuses on two threat vectors: PE-backed fire/security consolidators (Convergint, Pye-Barker, Everon, Pavion) entering IPS's markets, and the Edwards/Kidde ownership disruption weakening IPS's product moat. A third competitive front has emerged from research into IPS's electrical contractor customer base: **PE-driven consolidation of the $255 billion U.S. electrical contractor industry, with vertical integration into fire alarm, security, and life safety services**.

This third front is distinct from traditional competitive rivalry because it originates from IPS's own customer base. Forty-three percent of IPS's revenue flows through EC subcontractor relationships. The companies paying IPS are themselves being acquired by PE and building the capability to replace IPS.

### New Competitive Threats from EC Vertical Integration

Four EC-originated competitors now represent direct or emerging threats to IPS's market position:

**ArchKey Technologies (26North Partners, $1B+ platform) --- CRITICAL THREAT.** ArchKey is the parent company of PEC Solutions, one of IPS's top EC customers ($2.5M in three-year revenue). ArchKey is actively building a dedicated national fire/life safety team and hiring NICET-certified estimators. This is not a future possibility --- it is an active capability buildout. When operational, ArchKey's fire/security division will give PEC Solutions the option to perform fire alarm work in-house rather than subcontracting to IPS. The economic incentive is clear: ArchKey captures both the installation margin and the 10-15% subcontractor markup it currently pays to coordinate IPS's work.

**EMCOR Group (NYSE: EME, Fortune 500, $14.5B+ revenue) --- HIGH THREAT.** EMCOR is the parent company of Eagle Electrical, an IPS EC customer ($1.3M in three-year revenue). EMCOR already operates fire/life safety subsidiaries, including CRT, which employs hundreds of NICET-certified technicians. Eagle Electrical's current use of IPS as a fire alarm subcontractor may reflect local operational convenience rather than strategic necessity. A platform-level directive to use internal life safety resources would eliminate the IPS relationship immediately.

**IES Holdings (NASDAQ: IESC, ~$2.8B revenue) --- MODERATE THREAT.** IES Holdings is the parent company of KEP Electric, an IPS EC customer ($3.6M in three-year revenue). IES's Communications segment --- which includes fire alarm and security services --- grew 56% year-over-year in Q3 FY2025. As this segment scales, IES has both the incentive and the capability to bring fire/security work in-house across its EC subsidiaries.

**The State Group (Apollo Global Management) --- HIGH REGIONAL THREAT.** Apollo's backing of The State Group has produced a series of acquisitions across Kentucky, Indiana, and Illinois that explicitly include fire, security, and communications capabilities: Delta Services in Louisville KY, J&J Electric in Indiana, and Ruder Electric in Illinois. This is a vertical integration play targeting IPS's exact geography and service lines.

### The Displacement Mechanism

EC vertical integration threatens IPS through four specific vectors:

1. **Scope absorption** (18-36 months): ECs with internal fire/security capability eliminate the subcontractor scope that currently flows to IPS. On a $10M commercial project, fire alarm ($300K-$800K) and security ($200K-$500K) are absorbed into the EC's own P&L.

2. **Vendor list consolidation** (6-12 months): PE-backed EC platforms standardize subcontractor lists across all operating companies. IPS's local relationship with an EC subsidiary can be overridden by a platform-level decision to use a national fire/security provider (Convergint, Pye-Barker) that offers volume pricing.

3. **Bundle pricing** (12-24 months): EC platforms offer GCs a bundled electrical-plus-fire-plus-security package at lower total cost than separate subcontractor bids. Even if IPS's standalone price is competitive, the bundled package is simpler and cheaper for the GC.

4. **Post-construction capture** (24-48 months): The EC that manages fire/security installation has the as-built documentation, customer relationship, and building access to convert the project into recurring inspection, monitoring, and managed services contracts --- capturing the RMR that would otherwise flow to IPS.

### The Proof of Concept: APi Group

The EC-to-life-safety transformation has already been executed at scale. APi Group --- once a $2 billion electrical and specialty contractor --- acquired Chubb Fire & Security for $3.1 billion in 2022, transforming itself into a $7.9 billion life safety platform. APi's market capitalization roughly doubled post-acquisition, driven by the multiple re-rating that recurring fire/life safety revenue commands. APi did not build fire/security capability over decades. It bought it in a single transaction. This is the template that ArchKey, IES Holdings, and Apollo/State Group are following.

### Strategic Implication

The EC vertical integration threat compounds every competitive challenge identified earlier in this analysis. PE encirclement of IPS's markets is no longer a single-vector threat (fire/security consolidators entering from the outside) --- it is a two-vector threat that also includes IPS's own customer base building capability to displace it from the inside. The 23% of IPS revenue flowing through the EC subcontractor channel is the most vulnerable revenue stream in the portfolio. IPS must accelerate direct customer relationship development, reduce EC channel dependency from 43% toward 30% or below within 36 months, and prioritize post-construction service contract capture on every project installed through the EC channel. The window for this transition is 2-3 years, not 5 --- EC platforms can acquire fire/security capability in 90 days, the same speed at which they acquired their electrical businesses.
