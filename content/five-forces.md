# Porter's Five Forces Analysis — Integrated Protection Services (IPS)

**Capstone Strategic Analysis | March 2026**
**Team: Choi, Eger, McKean, Peat, Tucker**

---

## Executive Summary

The commercial fire alarm and security integration industry is **structurally attractive** for established players like IPS. Code-mandated demand creates a non-discretionary revenue floor. Switching costs — especially on fire alarm panel ecosystems — lock in customers for years. Substitution is effectively zero for fire and life-safety services. The primary structural threat is **competitive rivalry**, rated HIGH due to the collision of extreme market fragmentation with aggressive PE-driven consolidation. IPS's overall industry attractiveness assessment is **MODERATE-HIGH**: the demand fundamentals are strong, margins are improving through recurring revenue, and entry barriers protect incumbents — but the intensity of rivalry from well-capitalized national competitors requires active strategic positioning.

| Force | Rating | Key Driver |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Threat of New Entrants | LOW-MODERATE | State licensing, NICET certification, AHJ relationships |
| Supplier Power | **MODERATE-HIGH** | Edwards sold to Lone Star ($3B); Brivo/Eagle Eye merger; Verkada frenemy |
| Buyer Power | MODERATE | Varies by segment; fire alarm lock-in reduces leverage |
| Threat of Substitutes | LOW-MODERATE | Zero for fire/life-safety; moderate for electronic security |
| Competitive Rivalry | HIGH | Fragmented market under aggressive PE consolidation |

## Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — LOW-MODERATE

### Rating Justification

Entry barriers are high on the fire and life-safety side of the industry and moderate on the electronic security side, creating a split-market dynamic.

### Key Drivers

**State licensing creates hard legal barriers.** All three of IPS's home states require fire alarm contractor licensing with no legal alternative. Ohio mandates state fire alarm contractor licensing through the State Fire Marshal under ORC 3737.65. Kentucky requires a state license plus NICET Level II minimum for inspectors; operating without a license is a Class A misdemeanor under KRS 198B.620. Tennessee requires separate license classifications for Fire, Burglar, CCTV, and Monitoring services, with a $500 company certification fee and qualifying agent examinations.

**NICET certification creates a 10-year experience moat.** Progressing from NICET Level I to Level IV requires a minimum of 10 years of progressive experience. Level IV candidates must author a solo narrative essay on a complex fire alarm project. Ongoing continuing professional development and recertification every three years add sustained requirements. IPS holds NICET Level IV — the highest level available — which is achieved by a small subset of industry firms.

**AHJ relationship stickiness is an informal but powerful barrier.** Fire alarm systems require plan review and approval by local fire marshals and building departments. Established integrators build deep trust relationships over years of compliant work. New entrants must build credibility project-by-project, with no shortcut available. This effect is especially powerful in institutional markets such as healthcare, education, and government facilities.

**Manufacturer authorization requires proven capability.** Becoming an authorized dealer for major fire panel manufacturers — Edwards/EST, Notifier, Simplex — requires demonstrating technical capacity, financial stability, and service infrastructure. This creates a chicken-and-egg barrier: new firms cannot get authorized without demonstrated capability, but demonstrating capability requires working with authorized systems.

**Capital requirements are meaningful but not prohibitive.** Fleet vehicles, specialized testing equipment, inventory, insurance, bonding, and office space represent significant startup costs. Fire and life-safety work carries higher insurance premiums than general contracting due to life-safety liability. Surety bonds are required in many jurisdictions.

### The Split-Market Dynamic

The fire and life-safety side of the industry has **HIGH** entry barriers: licensing, NICET certification, AHJ relationships, and manufacturer authorization create multiple reinforcing barriers. EST/Edwards actively resists giving electrical contractors direct access to the fire alarm line — which limits EC encroachment on the fire side (Pete Keller, March 2025: "Electrical contractors can install to some extent, but they're not experts in terms of the fire alarm system. It's very code driven."). EC encroachment is a **LOW-MODERATE** threat on fire but **MODERATE-HIGH** on security, where ECs are spinning up low-voltage divisions for cameras and access control. The electronic security side has **MODERATE** barriers: Ohio does not require a statewide security license, IP-based systems lower technical requirements, and cloud platforms like Verkada simplify deployment for IT-oriented entrants.

**PE platforms bypass traditional entry barriers through acquisition.** While organic entry barriers remain high, PE-backed consolidators circumvent licensing, NICET certification, and AHJ relationship barriers entirely by acquiring existing licensed firms rather than building capabilities from scratch. Convergint's acquisition of Digital Visions in Cincinnati (June 2025) instantly gave Convergint a licensed, credentialed presence in IPS's home market without navigating any of the traditional entry barriers. Pye-Barker completed 57 acquisitions in 2025 alone, systematically buying its way into new markets at scale. With $680 million in undrawn debt capacity, Pye-Barker has substantial firepower for continued acquisition-driven entry into any geography it targets [102, 103].

### Strategic Implication for IPS

IPS's NICET IV certification, multi-state fire alarm licensing (OH, KY, TN), and 25+ years of AHJ relationships form a durable competitive moat on the fire and life-safety side. The security side is more exposed to new entrants, particularly cloud-native competitors like Verkada. Critically, IPS's combined fire-plus-security capability is itself a barrier — few new entrants can offer both simultaneously. This bundling creates a natural defense against entry.

**PE-backed electrical contractors represent a new and distinct entry vector.** Beyond fire/security PE platforms (Convergint, Pye-Barker) acquiring their way into IPS's markets, a parallel entry mechanism is emerging from the $255 billion electrical contracting industry. In 2024, 79 EC acquisitions were completed (73% PE-driven), and the consolidated EC platforms are vertically integrating into fire alarm, security, and life safety. APi Group --- once a $2 billion EC --- acquired Chubb Fire & Security for $3.1 billion, transforming itself into a $7.9 billion life safety platform. ArchKey Technologies (26North Partners, $1B+ platform), parent of IPS customer PEC Solutions, is actively building a national fire/life safety team and hiring NICET-certified estimators. EMCOR Group (NYSE: EME, Fortune 500), parent of IPS customer Eagle Electrical, already operates fire/life safety subsidiaries employing hundreds of NICET-certified technicians. IES Holdings (NASDAQ: IESC), parent of IPS customer KEP Electric, grew its fire/security communications segment 56% year-over-year. Apollo-backed State Group is acquiring fire, security, and communications companies across Kentucky, Indiana, and Tennessee --- IPS's exact geography. EC entry into fire/security is uniquely threatening because ECs bypass traditional barriers through a different mechanism than acquisition: they are already inside the building during construction, already hold the GC relationship, and already wire the low-voltage backbone. The step from "electrical subcontractor" to "fire/security provider" is shorter than the step from "national PE platform" to "local market entrant." This entry vector elevates the Threat of New Entrants toward MODERATE, particularly on the security side where licensing barriers are lower than for fire alarm.

## Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE

### Rating Justification

Supplier power is high on the fire alarm panel side due to market concentration and proprietary ecosystems, but low to moderate on the electronic security side due to open standards and competitive distribution channels.

### Key Drivers

**The fire alarm panel market is highly concentrated.** The top five manufacturers control approximately 80% or more of the commercial market. Johnson Controls (Simplex, Autocall, Zettler) holds an estimated 20–25% U.S. share. Edwards/EST (Carrier Global) holds 13–17% globally. Honeywell/Notifier holds an estimated 15–20% with a $5.4 billion fire safety division and $290 million in annual R&D. Bosch holds 18–22% globally. Siemens (Cerberus PRO, Sinteso) holds 11–14% globally.

**Switching costs for fire panels are very high.** Fire alarm panels, devices, and software operate in proprietary manufacturer-specific ecosystems. Switching manufacturers requires complete system redesign, re-permitting, technician retraining, and device replacement. This creates lock-in for both the integrator and the end customer. Once an integrator standardizes on a panel line, it builds expertise, inventory, and customer base around that platform — creating mutual dependency.

**Access control is more open, reducing supplier power.** Genetec uses non-proprietary HID Mercury controllers, allowing customers to switch software without hardware replacement. Brivo's cloud platform is open and partner-friendly. The ONVIF standard in video surveillance enables camera interoperability across VMS platforms. This openness gives integrators significantly more leverage against access control and video manufacturers than against fire panel makers.

**The Brivo-Eagle Eye merger concentrates IPS's XaaS platform dependency.** IPS's two primary cloud platform partners — Brivo for ACaaS and Eagle Eye Networks for VSaaS — merged on December 29, 2025, creating a unified AI cloud-native security platform operating under the Brivo name. This simplifies IPS's technology stack but concentrates strategic risk. If Brivo changes partner terms, pricing, or program structure, IPS has limited alternatives without incurring significant migration costs.

**The distribution channel is competitive.** ADI (Resideo), WESCO/Anixter, and Graybar compete actively for integrator business. No single distributor has pricing power over integrators. Online ordering and direct manufacturer sales provide additional alternatives.

**Consolidation shifts manufacturer pricing power against independents.** Convergint at $2.7 billion in revenue and Pye-Barker at $1.0 billion negotiate volume pricing from fire panel manufacturers — Edwards/EST, Notifier, Simplex — that IPS at approximately $20–30 million in revenue cannot match. As consolidation continues and national platforms capture an increasing share of manufacturer revenue, manufacturers may increasingly favor large national accounts with preferred pricing tiers and dedicated technical support, widening the cost gap between PE-backed platforms and independent integrators [102, 103].

### Strategic Implication for IPS

IPS faces moderate supplier power overall — high on the fire panel side (proprietary lock-in is mutual), lower on the security and video side (open standards). However, two major ownership changes in late 2024 have materially increased supplier risk:

**Edwards/Kidde Ownership Change (HIGH IMPACT):** Carrier sold its Edwards fire alarm business to Lone Star Funds for $3 billion (closed December 2, 2024). Edwards, Kidde, GST, and Aritech now operate under Kidde Global Solutions. IPS is the largest privately-held EST/Edwards distributor nationally (~$6M/year, 95% of fire alarm work). Under new PE ownership, Edwards is adding distributor partners in IPS territories — directly weakening IPS's exclusivity. Pete Keller (CFO, March 2025): the Edwards partnership is "not as strong as it used to be." Simultaneously, Honeywell acquired Carrier's LenelS2, Onity, and Supra access control businesses for $4.95 billion. Both of IPS's two most important OEM relationships changed ownership within months. This represents the most significant supplier risk IPS has faced in its 25-year history.

**Verkada as Frenemy (MODERATE IMPACT):** Verkada ($4.5 billion valuation, 2025) operates a direct-to-customer model for cloud video and access control, bypassing integrators like IPS. IPS confirmed it does NOT sell Verkada products. Pete Keller described Verkada as "playing both sides" — selling direct to end users while also trying to recruit integrators as channel partners. Additionally, Anixter/Wesco (major distributor) is attempting to work directly with end users and bring IPS in as a subcontractor, creating friction in the distribution channel.

IPS should: (1) diversify fire alarm partnerships to reduce Edwards dependency — evaluate Notifier (Honeywell), Simplex (JCI), or other fire panel OEMs for secondary capability; (2) continue to avoid Verkada and leverage the bundled fire+security model as a competitive differentiator against Verkada's security-only offering; (3) monitor Brivo partner terms carefully and maintain Genetec, LenelS2, and Milestone as secondary cloud platform options.

## Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE

### Rating Justification

Buyer power varies significantly by market segment, from low in compliance-driven verticals (healthcare, education) to high in commercially competitive segments (office, manufacturing). Fire alarm switching costs structurally reduce buyer leverage across all segments.

### Buyer Power by Segment

| Segment | Power Level | Key Dynamic |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Healthcare** | LOW | Code-mandated systems (NFPA, CMS, Joint Commission); compliance-driven procurement; preference for single-vendor solutions; long decision cycles |
| **Education (K-12 / Higher Ed)** | MODERATE | Budget-constrained but grant-funded (BSCA, SVPP, DHS); public competitive bidding increases leverage; post-incident urgency reduces price sensitivity |
| **Banking / Financial** | MODERATE-HIGH | National standardization requirements; volume leverage across branch networks; partially offset by installed-base switching costs |
| **Manufacturing / Logistics** | MODERATE-HIGH | More price-sensitive; insurance and code compliance driven; shorter decision cycles; ROI-oriented |
| **Commercial Office** | HIGH | Competitive bidding common; hybrid work reduced demand; tenant improvement work is project-based; high price sensitivity |
| **Multi-Tenant Housing** | MODERATE | Growing segment; access control complexity increasing; moderate price sensitivity |

### Switching Cost Analysis

The nature and cost of switching varies dramatically by system type, which fundamentally shapes buyer leverage:

| System Type | Switching Cost | Lock-In Mechanism |
|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Fire alarm | VERY HIGH | Proprietary panel ecosystem; full replacement required to change manufacturers |
| Access control (on-premise) | HIGH | Hardware + credential database + user configuration migration |
| Access control (cloud/ACaaS) | MODERATE | Cloud platforms easier to migrate; credential portability improving |
| Video surveillance | MODERATE-LOW | IP cameras increasingly ONVIF-interoperable; cloud VMS creates subscription lock-in |
| Central station monitoring | LOW (technical) / MODERATE (contractual) | 3–5 year contract terms create contractual lock-in |
| Service/maintenance contracts | MODERATE | Technician familiarity with installed systems; customer inertia |

### Strategic Implication for IPS

IPS's diverse segment mix — healthcare, education, banking, manufacturing — hedges against concentrated buyer power in any single vertical. Fire alarm installed base creates the strongest customer lock-in: once IPS installs a system, the switching cost for the customer is very high. IPS should prioritize fire alarm as the "anchor" service that creates long-term customer relationships, then cross-sell security, access control, and managed services to the same customer base at lower buyer resistance.

## Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — LOW-MODERATE

### Rating Justification

Substitution threat is effectively zero for code-mandated fire and life-safety services and moderate for electronic security services. IPS's bundled service model provides a natural defense against security-side substitution.

### Substitution by Service Line

| IPS Service | Substitute Threat | Substitute Source | Rating |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Fire alarm | NONE | Required by NFPA 72 and local codes; no occupancy certificate without it | ZERO |
| Fire suppression / special hazard | NONE | Required by NFPA 13, NFPA 17, and local codes | ZERO |
| ERCES/DAS | NONE | Increasingly mandated for new construction; highly specialized | ZERO |
| Nurse call / intercom | VERY LOW | Required in healthcare (CMS, Joint Commission) | NEAR-ZERO |
| Mass notification | LOW | Code-mandated in certain occupancy types | LOW |
| Access control | MODERATE | Verkada direct sales; IT self-management; cloud-native platforms | MODERATE |
| Video surveillance | MODERATE | Verkada ($350M+ revenue); DIY prosumer (Ring for Business); IT teams | MODERATE |
| Intrusion detection | MODERATE | DIY commercial (SimpliSafe); IT-managed solutions | MODERATE |
| Weapons detection | LOW | Highly specialized; requires integration expertise | LOW |

### Key Substitute Competitors

**Verkada** is the most significant substitute threat on the security side. With over $350 million in 2024 revenue and a $3.2 billion valuation, Verkada offers a cloud-only, closed ecosystem targeting IT departments that want simplicity. The platform operates without RTSP, ONVIF, or video APIs. Verkada initially bypassed integrators entirely but now has a channel partner program. Critically, Verkada does not address fire alarm or life-safety needs.

**DIY/Prosumer solutions** (Ring for Business, SimpliSafe) serve only the low-end commercial market. They cannot replace code-mandated systems and are limited to small retail and small office applications.

**In-house IT teams** can manage IP video and basic access control but cannot substitute for fire alarm, suppression, ERCES, or weapons detection, which require NICET certification, state licensing, and AHJ coordination.

### Strategic Implication for IPS

IPS's strongest defense against substitution is its fire and life-safety portfolio — these services are legally required and cannot be self-served, outsourced to IT, or replaced by cloud platforms. The electronic security side faces real substitution pressure from Verkada and IT self-management. IPS's strategy of bundling fire-plus-security is a natural hedge: customers who need IPS for code-mandated fire alarm frequently add security, access control, and video under the same contract for simplicity and single-vendor accountability. This bundling reduces substitution risk on the security side by embedding security services within a fire-anchored customer relationship.

## Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

### Rating Justification

The industry is simultaneously highly fragmented (5,000+ fire/life-safety companies nationwide) and rapidly consolidating through aggressive PE-driven M&A. This creates a competitive squeeze in IPS's specific markets.

### Key Drivers

**Extreme fragmentation persists.** Over 5,000 fire and life-safety companies operate nationwide, with thousands of additional electronic security firms. Most are small, local, independent operators. This fragmentation sustains price competition on project-based work.

**PE-driven consolidation is accelerating.** Through August 2024, 119 M&A transactions were recorded in the fire and security integration space, with 38.9% targeting fire and life-safety companies. The pace is remarkable: Pye-Barker completed 57 acquisitions in 2025 (up from 57 in 2024). Convergint completed 10+ add-on acquisitions in 2024–2025. Everon has completed 8 acquisitions since its $1.61 billion LBO in October 2023. Sciens completed 16 acquisitions in six years. Pavion completed 23+ since 2020. PitchBook data reveals combined PE-backed competitor debt exceeding $9.6 billion — Convergint ($2.70B), Pye-Barker ($4.31B), and Everon ($1.61B+) — providing massive acquisition war chests. PE investment vintage cycles suggest heavy M&A activity will continue through 2026 and beyond [102, 103, 106, 107, 108, 109].

**Industry profitability is improving but unevenly distributed.** Over 60% of SDM Top 100 integrators reported profit increases in 2024, averaging 8% above 2023 levels. Project-based work yields typical gross margins of 15–25%, while service and recurring revenue (RMR) work yields 50–70% gross margins. The shift to recurring revenue is improving overall industry profitability.

**Price competition is common on project work.** Competitive bidding is standard in government, education, and commercial office segments. Differentiation through service quality, response time, and specialization is the primary path to premium pricing. RMR and managed services reduce exposure to per-project price sensitivity.

**Geographic overlap is intensifying in IPS's markets.** IPS faces growing direct competition in every market:

| IPS Market | Competitors Present |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Cincinnati** | Silco (HQ), Convergint (acquired Digital Visions Jun 2025) [102], Pavion (via Turnkey Technology) [104], Pye-Barker (acquired Shiver Security, Mason OH) [103] |
| **Columbus** | Silco, Paladin (expanding), Everon [106] |
| **Kentucky** | Pye-Barker (Modern Systems in Somerset, Care Security in Louisville) [103], Everon (Louisville) [106] |
| **Nashville** | Convergint (acquired Fiber Solutions, May 2025) [102] |

**A single PE firm controls the two largest platforms.** PitchBook data reveals that Leonard Green & Partners holds majority stakes in both Convergint Technologies ($2.7 billion revenue) and Pye-Barker Fire & Safety ($1.0 billion revenue, valued at $6.1 billion). Combined, one PE firm controls approximately $3.7 billion of fire and security integration revenue and 19,000 employees. The January 2025 Pye-Barker recapitalization brought sovereign wealth fund investors — Abu Dhabi Investment Authority and GIC (Singapore) — into the fire/security sector at a $6.1 billion valuation, signaling institutional conviction in the industry's long-term durability [102, 103, 110].

### Strategic Implication for IPS

Rivalry is the most threatening structural force for IPS. The combination of extreme fragmentation (price competition on every project bid) and rapid PE consolidation (well-capitalized nationals entering IPS's home markets) creates a competitive squeeze. IPS must differentiate on service quality, technical expertise (NICET IV), fire-plus-security bundling, and local responsiveness --- attributes that PE roll-ups often struggle to maintain after acquisition. The accelerating shift to recurring revenue through XaaS offerings reduces IPS's exposure to project-by-project price competition and increases business resilience.

**EC platform vertical integration intensifies rivalry from a new direction.** Competitive rivalry in IPS's markets is no longer limited to fire/security integrators competing against each other. The $255 billion electrical contracting industry --- IPS's primary customer channel --- is undergoing its own PE-driven consolidation (79 acquisitions in 2024, 73% PE-driven), and the consolidated EC platforms are vertically integrating into fire alarm, security, and life safety. This adds a new competitive dimension: IPS's own customers becoming competitors. Three of IPS's top eight EC customers are already PE- or corporate-owned (ArchKey/PEC Solutions, EMCOR/Eagle Electrical, IES Holdings/KEP Electric), and all three parent companies have active fire/security strategies. The displacement mechanism --- scope absorption, vendor list consolidation, bundle pricing, and post-construction RMR capture --- puts $3.8-6.3 million in annual IPS revenue at risk over a 3-5 year horizon. APi Group's transformation from a $2 billion EC to a $7.9 billion life safety platform via the $3.1 billion Chubb acquisition demonstrates that EC-to-fire/security vertical integration can happen at massive scale and speed. Apollo-backed State Group is executing the same playbook across Kentucky, Indiana, and Tennessee with acquisitions of Delta Services, J&J Electric, and Ruder Electric --- all with fire, security, and communications capability in IPS's geography. The convergence of fire/security PE consolidation (outside-in) and EC vertical integration (inside-out) creates a competitive squeeze that is qualitatively more intense than either force alone.

## Overall Industry Assessment

The commercial fire alarm and security integration industry is structurally attractive for established players with IPS's profile. The demand floor created by code-mandated services is unmatched in most industries — buildings literally cannot open without fire alarm systems. Switching costs protect customer relationships. Entry barriers protect market position. The primary threat is competitive rivalry driven by PE consolidation, but this force also creates opportunity: as PE roll-ups acquire and integrate companies in IPS's markets, they often experience service disruption, technician turnover, and customer relationship erosion. IPS can position itself as the high-quality, locally-accountable alternative that PE-assembled platforms struggle to replicate.
