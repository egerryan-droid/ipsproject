# The Glass House — Operational Reality Behind the Growth Story

**Xavier EMBA Capstone | Internal Strategic Analysis | May 2026**
**Team: Choi, Eger, McKean, Peat, Tucker**

---

## Executive Summary

IPS grew revenue 26% in 2025 and ended the year with negative cash. A company that added $11M in revenue finished with -$71K in the bank and $1.7M drawn on its line of credit. The revenue growth is real. It is masking deterioration in almost every operational health metric.

This analysis reframes the internal assessment by looking past the revenue headline to examine what is happening inside the business. Eleven metrics tell the story: while revenue and gross margin improved, EBITDA margin declined, cash went negative, DSO spiked 22 days, bad debt exploded 94x, SGA consumed 42 cents of every new revenue dollar, customer concentration accelerated to 46.4% in the top three accounts, backlog failed to grow despite 26% revenue growth, customer retention held flat at 81.5% (losing 1 in 5 customers annually), and RMR as a percentage of revenue barely moved from its industry-trailing 5.8%.

The glass house thesis is not that IPS is failing --- it is that IPS's technical excellence and revenue growth have outpaced the operational infrastructure required to sustain that growth under competitive stress. Revenue scales with project wins. Collections, cost control, customer quality, cash management, recurring revenue development, and operational process maturity do not scale at the same rate because they require systematic investment that project-focused leadership defers. The competitive stress from PE-backed platforms is arriving faster than the infrastructure is being built. The house is glass. The question is whether IPS reinforces the foundation before the stones start flying.

## What Happened in 2025, Line by Line

### Gross Profit: Real Improvement

| Metric | 2024 | 2025 | Change |
|--------|------|------|--------|
| Gross margin | 31.9% | 35.6% | +3.7pp |
| Gross profit dollars | $13.6M | $19.1M | +$5.5M |

The gross margin improvement is real, driven by direct labor leverage on the "parts and smarts" model. As revenue grows, IPS's labor cost (only 10% of project cost) does not grow proportionally. Direct labor as a percentage of COGS declined from 34.5% in 2021 to 24.9% in 2025. This is a genuine structural advantage that the model correctly identifies.

### SGA: Exploded

| Component | 2024 | 2025 | Change | % of SGA Increase |
|-----------|------|------|--------|-------------------|
| Admin salaries | $4.6M | $7.1M | +$2.5M (+54%) | 55% of increase |
| Bad debt | $9K | $849K | +$840K (94x) | 18% of increase |
| Health insurance | $456K | $891K | +$435K (+95%) | 9% of increase |
| Auto/lease | --- | --- | +$264K | 6% of increase |
| All other SGA | --- | --- | +$561K | 12% of increase |
| **Total SGA** | **$8.9M** | **$13.5M** | **+$4.6M (+52%)** | **100%** |

IPS spent 42 cents of every new revenue dollar on SGA. Revenue grew 26%. SGA grew 52%. This is negative operating leverage --- the opposite of what a scaling company should produce.

The SGA spike breaks into two categories:
- **Structural (~$3.6M):** Salaries, health insurance, vehicles --- these represent hiring from approximately 120 to 140 employees and do not reverse
- **Potentially one-time (~$840K):** Bad debt write-off --- either a single material customer default or systemic collections deterioration

Even normalizing for the bad debt spike, structural SGA grew approximately $3.6M (40%) on 26% revenue growth. The back office is growing faster than the business it supports.

### Net Effect: Negative Operating Leverage

| Metric | Growth Rate | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Revenue | +26% | Strong |
| Gross profit | +40% | Very strong |
| SGA | +52% | **Outpaced revenue** |
| EBITDA | +19% | **Lagged revenue** |
| EBITDA margin | -0.7pp (11.4% to 10.7%) | **Declined** |

The company got better at making money on projects and worse at controlling what it costs to run the business. Gross margin expansion of 3.7 percentage points was more than consumed by SGA expansion of 4.3 percentage points.

### Working Capital Ate the Cash

IPS added $11M in revenue but tied up $6.3M in uncollected receivables. The company earned $5.2M in net income but ended with negative cash because distributions ($3.7M) and working capital growth consumed all earnings plus existing cash.

## The SGA Explosion: What $13.5M Buys

SGA at 25.1% of revenue represents $13.5M spent on running the business rather than delivering projects. This is the single largest threat to the financial model's projections, which assume SGA declining to 21% by 2030.

### The Structural Reality

If SGA remains at 24.5% (a slight improvement from 25.1%) rather than declining to the model's 21%, the cumulative impact is severe:

| Year | Model SGA | Structural SGA | Annual Gap | Cumulative |
|------|----------|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| 2027 | $17.2M | $18.4M | $1.2M | $1.2M |
| 2028 | $18.4M | $20.2M | $1.8M | $3.0M |
| 2029 | $20.1M | $22.6M | $2.5M | $5.5M |
| 2030 | $21.5M | $24.6M | $3.1M | $8.6M |

Cumulative EBITDA gap: $8.6M over four years. At an 8x EBITDA multiple: $70M less enterprise value. This single assumption --- SGA trajectory --- swings the partner buyout value by $29M on the 42% stake.

### What Drove the Spike

The data points to three drivers:

1. **Hiring ahead of growth:** IPS grew from approximately 120 to 140 employees (17% headcount growth) to support 26% revenue growth. The hiring included administrative and management roles, not just field technicians, driving the 54% salary increase that outpaced revenue growth by 2x.

2. **Bad debt crystallization:** The $849K bad debt expense (up from $9K) represents either a material customer default or a systemic deterioration in collections discipline at 122-day DSO. At 1.6% of revenue written off, this is high for a commercial contractor.

3. **Branch expansion costs:** Nashville and Lexington branches carry fixed costs on undersized revenue bases. Lexington operates at 60.8% expense ratio on $297K in YTD revenue --- effectively a startup within the company consuming SGA dollars without proportional return.

## The Working Capital Trap

### DSO Analysis

| Year | Revenue | AR | DSO | Change |
|------|---------|-----|-----|--------|
| 2024 | $42.7M | $11.7M | 100 days | Baseline |
| 2025 | $53.8M | $18.0M | 122 days | +22 days |

DSO spiked 22 days in one year. The $6.3M AR increase consumed 57% of the $11M in new revenue. IPS is doing the work, paying its people, buying materials, and then waiting four months to get paid.

### Why DSO Is Structural, Not Fixable

DSO is driven by revenue channel mix, not collection effort:

| Channel | Payment Cycle | 2025 Revenue Share | Trend |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|-------|
| EC/GC subcontract | 90-150 days (retainage) | 43% | **Growing** |
| National accounts (Amazon) | 60-90 days | 24% | Stable |
| Direct end-user | 45-75 days | 15% | Stable |
| Service/recurring | 30-45 days | 18% | Growing slowly |

The fastest-growing channel (EC at 43%) has the longest payment cycle (90-150 days). As EC share grows, DSO structurally worsens regardless of collection effort. The model's assumption of DSO improving from 122 to 75 days requires shifting the revenue mix away from EC --- the opposite of current trajectory.

### The Cash Arithmetic

At 122-day DSO on $54M revenue, IPS carries $18M in accounts receivable. At the model's 75-day target, AR would be $11M. The $7M difference is the working capital penalty for the current channel mix --- $7M in cash trapped in receivables that could otherwise fund the partner buyout, managed services infrastructure, or an acquisition.

## The Backlog Problem

| Year | Revenue Growth | Backlog at Year-End |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| 2024 | Baseline | $11.3M |
| 2025 | +26% | $11.3M |

Backlog was $11.3M at end of 2024. After 26% revenue growth, backlog at end of 2025: $11.3M. Exactly the same.

In a project-based business, backlog is the leading indicator of next year's revenue. IPS consumed its backlog without replenishing it proportionally. This means one of two things:

1. **2025 pulled revenue forward.** Projects that would have been 2026 revenue were executed in 2025, meaning 2026 will be softer than modeled.
2. **Pipeline development is not keeping pace with execution.** IPS executes faster than it sells. The 20 salespeople ("more farming than hunting") are not generating enough new pipeline to replace consumed backlog.

The model projects $60.8M for 2026 (+13%). If backlog has not expanded, the additional $7M must materialize from pipeline that does not yet appear in the backlog. The model assumes this happens. The backlog data does not confirm it.

## The Bad Debt Signal

| Year | Bad Debt Expense | % of Revenue |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| 2024 | $9K | 0.02% |
| 2025 | $849K | 1.58% |

$9K to $849K represents a 94x increase. At 1.6% of revenue written off, this is elevated for a commercial contractor with institutional customers.

Two interpretations:
- **Single customer default:** One large customer (possibly in the EC chain with extended payment terms) defaulted on a material balance
- **Systemic collections deterioration:** At 122-day DSO, IPS is chasing receivables harder and collecting less. The bad debt is the tip of the aging AR iceberg.

Both interpretations are concerning. If it is a single default, IPS's concentration risk ($46.4% in top three customers) makes this repeatable. If it is systemic, the working capital trap is worse than the DSO alone suggests.

## The Retention Fracture

81.5% retention = approximately 185 customers churning annually out of approximately 1,000.

### The Quality Degradation Hypothesis

If churning customers are building owners with direct relationships (RMR-convertible) and replacements are EC subcontractor projects (one-time, no RMR), IPS is slowly trading its most valuable customer relationships for its least valuable ones.

Evidence supporting this hypothesis:
- Customer count grew (975 to 1,038) but RMR barely moved (5.8%)
- Revenue per customer grew ($33.6K to $51.8K) driven by larger projects, not deeper relationships
- New customer acquisition is declining (220 to 173) while EC channel is growing (31% to 43%)
- Service agreements are growing in dollars but the overall portfolio is shifting toward project-heavy, service-light

The count stays the same. The quality degrades. The RMR needle does not move despite adding customers --- this supports the degradation interpretation.

## The Unifying Pattern

Revenue scales with project wins. Everything else --- collections, cost control, customer quality, cash management, recurring revenue development, and operational process maturity --- does not scale at the same rate. These operational dimensions require systematic investment that project-focused leadership defers because the next project win always feels more urgent than the next process improvement.

The EOS implementation (18 months old) is an attempt to address this. It is directionally correct. But EOS at 18 months has produced a process maturity rating of 2/5, no documented standard operating procedures, no training programs, no succession plans, no CRM, and no subscription billing infrastructure. IPS is running a $55M operation with management systems still being built.

## The Distributions Problem

| Year | Net Income | Distributions | Cash Impact |
|------|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| 2025 | $5.2M | $3.7M | $3.7M removed from business |
| At year-end | --- | --- | Cash position: -$71K |

IPS distributed $3.7M to owners from a business that ended the year with negative cash. This is not illegal or unusual for a privately-held company. But it reveals a capital allocation choice: leadership prioritized owner distributions over operational investment, working capital, or cash reserves.

At $3.7M per year in distributions from a cash-negative business, IPS is simultaneously:
- Underfunding the RMR infrastructure buildout
- Underfunding the ERP modernization
- Underfunding training and development
- Carrying no cash buffer against customer loss or competitive disruption
- Hoping the growth trajectory generates enough future cash to cover all deferred investments

The distributions are the most immediate lever for improving the financial position. Reducing distributions by $2M per year creates a $2M annual investment fund without requiring any revenue growth, margin improvement, or operational change. Whether leadership is willing to make this trade is a strategic question, not a financial one.

## What This Means for Recommendations

Every scenario modeled --- Amazon loss, EC capture, Edwards dilution, forced buyout --- assumed an underlying business that was operationally sound and simply hit by external events. The glass house thesis says the foundation is already stressed. External shocks do not have to be dramatic to cause serious problems because the operational buffer is thin:

| Buffer | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Cash | -$71K (no cushion) |
| DSO | 122 days (working capital trap) |
| SGA | 25.1% and growing (cost discipline gap) |
| Backlog | Flat (pipeline uncertainty) |
| Bad debt | $849K (collection/credit risk) |
| Distributions | $3.7M from cash-negative business (capital allocation) |

The model projects all of these improving. 2025 showed all of them worsening. The model needs to explain what changes operationally to reverse these trends, not just assume they mean-revert.

## The Honest Framing

IPS has built an impressive revenue engine on the back of genuine technical excellence. The NICET IV capability, the combined fire and security breadth, the 25-year customer relationships, the organic growth from $28M to $54M without a single acquisition --- these are real achievements that reflect real capability.

But the company has not yet built the operational infrastructure to sustain that engine under competitive stress. Revenue grew 26% and cash went negative. SGA grew 52% on 26% revenue. EBITDA margin declined despite gross margin expansion. The backlog did not refill. Bad debt exploded. And the competitive environment is accelerating against every one of these weaknesses simultaneously.

The house is glass. The stones are already in the air. The question is not whether IPS needs to reinforce the foundation. The question is whether there is time to do it before the competitive stress arrives in full force --- and the PE/EC consolidation intelligence says the stress is not arriving in 2-3 years. It is here now. Convergint acquired Digital Visions in Cincinnati in June 2025. Pye-Barker acquired Cincinnati Alarm Systems in 2025. ArchKey is building a national fire team and hiring NICET estimators. Three of IPS's eight EC customers are already PE-owned.

The recommendation is not "grow faster." The recommendation is "grow differently" --- and do it while the revenue is still growing, the FCF is still positive (barely), and the partner is still patient.
