## Executive Summary

The fire and security integration industry has spent the last three years watching one threat: PE-backed consolidation of fire/security integrators themselves. Convergint, Pye-Barker, Everon, and Pavion have consumed over 200 companies since 2023 and deployed $9.6 billion in debt to do it. That threat is real, documented, and accelerating.

But there is a second consolidation wave --- less visible, larger in total capital deployed, and potentially more dangerous to IPS's business model --- happening in an adjacent industry: **electrical contracting**. Private equity is rolling up the $255 billion U.S. electrical contractor industry at a pace that now exceeds fire/security M&A activity. In 2024, 79 EC acquisitions were completed, 73% of them PE-driven. The marquee transactions --- Power Solutions International at $1.95 billion, ArchKey Technologies at over $1 billion, Miller Electric at $865 million --- are not small tuck-ins. They are platform-scale investments by sophisticated PE firms betting on a structural thesis: the electrification of the American economy.

This would be a parallel-universe problem for IPS if it stayed in the electrical lane. It does not. The consolidation thesis that drives EC rollups has a second chapter: **vertical integration into fire alarm, security, and life safety**. The economic logic is straightforward. Electrical contractors are already inside every commercial building during construction. They already hold the subcontractor relationships with general contractors. They already wire the low-voltage backbone on which fire alarm and security systems run. The step from "we wire the building" to "we install and maintain the fire alarm and security system" is shorter than the step from "we are a national PE platform" to "we open a local office in Cincinnati." And it comes with a prize that PE investors crave above all else: **recurring monitoring revenue (RMR)** from inspection, testing, and monitoring contracts that persist for the life of the building.

This is not hypothetical. APi Group --- once a $2 billion electrical contractor --- transformed itself into a **$7.911 billion life safety platform (TTM 2025) with a $21.25 billion enterprise value and 16.80x EV/EBITDA** through its $3.1 billion acquisition of Chubb Fire & Security and continued tuck-in acquisitions. EMCOR Group, a Fortune 500 electrical and mechanical contractor, operates fire/life safety subsidiaries that employ hundreds of NICET-certified technicians. IES Holdings, a publicly traded EC, grew its communications segment (fire alarm and security) by 56% year-over-year in Q3 FY2025. And ArchKey Technologies --- IPS's own customer through its PEC Solutions subsidiary --- is actively building a national fire/life safety team and hiring NICET-certified estimators.

For IPS, this is the "third front." The first front is PE consolidation of fire/security integrators (Convergint, Pye-Barker). The second front is the Edwards/Kidde ownership disruption weakening IPS's product moat. The third front is the industry that generates 43% of IPS's revenue --- electrical contractors --- being consolidated by private equity and vertically integrating into IPS's core service lines. Six of IPS's top eight EC customers are building or already have fire/security capability. The displacement mechanism is clear: as ECs build or acquire fire/security capability, the subcontractor relationship that currently channels $12.65 million annually to IPS gets absorbed into the EC's own P&L. This is not a forecast --- it is already happening. PEC Solutions, IPS's customer through ArchKey/26North, saw its revenue to IPS drop 89% (from $1.2 million to $130,000) as ArchKey's internal fire/security team came online. Revenue erosion across the EC channel is estimated at $3.8M-$8.0M over 3-5 years.

The window to address this threat is measured in years, not decades. The regional data is unambiguous: Apollo-backed State Group is acquiring fire, security, and communications companies across Kentucky, Indiana, and Tennessee --- IPS's exact geography. Convergint acquired Digital Visions in Cincinnati. Pye-Barker acquired Cincinnati Alarm Systems. The data center pipeline in Ohio alone --- Amazon ($23 billion), Google ($7.2 billion), Intel ($28 billion) --- will draw every PE-backed EC platform into the region. IPS must understand, quantify, and respond to this third front before the door to the building is bought by the company that used to hold it open.

## The Scale of the EC Rollup

The U.S. electrical contracting industry is a $255 billion market comprising approximately 219,000 businesses. It is one of the largest, most fragmented sectors in the American economy. The vast majority of EC firms are small --- local or regional, family-owned, operating with fewer than 50 employees. This fragmentation is the condition that PE capital exploits. Where fragmentation exists alongside strong end-market demand, PE sees an arbitrage: buy small companies at lower multiples (5-6x EBITDA), integrate them into a platform, and harvest the consolidated entity at higher multiples (8-10x or more).

In 2024, the EC rollup accelerated to 79 completed acquisitions. Of those, **58 were PE-driven --- 73% of all deal activity**. This is not a nascent trend. There are now **10 or more active PE-backed EC platform strategies** competing for targets, each with hundreds of millions or billions in committed capital. The pace of deal-making exceeds even the fire/security sector, where 129 deals were recorded in 2023 and 109 through September 2024.

### Why Electrical Contracting, Why Now

The EC rollup is powered by four structural demand drivers that are not cyclical:

**1. Data Center Construction.** The hyperscale data center buildout is the single largest construction demand driver in a generation. Amazon committed $23 billion to Ohio data centers. Google committed $7.2 billion. Intel's Ohio fab represents $28 billion. Meta's Prometheus facility in New Albany, Ohio will be the first data center to exceed 1 GW of power. Data centers are, fundamentally, electrical infrastructure projects --- they consume more electrical labor per square foot than any other building type. Every dollar of data center construction flows through electrical contractors first and longest.

**2. Electrification and Energy Transition.** The electrification of transportation (EV charging infrastructure), building systems (heat pumps replacing gas furnaces), and industrial processes creates structural demand growth for electrical work that is independent of the real estate cycle. Federal policy (Inflation Reduction Act, Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act) has committed hundreds of billions in spending that flows directly through the EC industry.

**3. Federal and State Infrastructure Spending.** The $1.2 trillion Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA), the CHIPS and Science Act, and state-level infrastructure programs are funding highway, bridge, water treatment, power grid, and broadband projects --- all of which require electrical contracting at scale. This spending is appropriated and committed, providing a multi-year demand floor.

**4. Skilled Labor Scarcity.** The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects 81,000 electrician openings per year through 2034, with 300,000 needed and 200,000 retiring. The NICET certification pipeline for fire alarm technicians takes a minimum of 10 years from Level I to Level IV. Labor scarcity is the condition that makes acquisitions rational: it is faster to buy an EC with 200 electricians than to recruit and train them. PE platforms solve the labor problem with capital, not classrooms.

These four demand drivers are mutually reinforcing. Data centers require electrification. Electrification requires federal spending. Federal spending requires labor. Labor scarcity drives consolidation. The flywheel is structural, not speculative.

## What Is Driving the Capital

### The Investment Thesis in Brief

Private equity's thesis on electrical contracting can be summarized in four propositions:

1. **Demand is structural and non-discretionary.** Buildings need electricity. Data centers need massive amounts of electricity. The electrification of the economy is a multi-decade trend backed by federal legislation, not just market sentiment.

2. **Fragmentation enables multiple arbitrage.** Buying a $10 million EC at 5-6x EBITDA and rolling it into a $500 million platform that trades at 8-10x creates immediate paper value. The arbitrage is mechanical and repeatable across hundreds of targets.

3. **Labor is the scarce resource, and acquisition is the fastest way to get it.** Training an electrician takes 4-5 years through an apprenticeship program. Buying an EC with 100 trained electricians takes 90 days. In a labor-constrained market, the only way to grow at PE-required speeds is to buy the labor force.

4. **Recurring revenue can be layered onto the construction base.** Electrical construction is project-based and cyclical. PE investors tolerate this because the construction base provides market access --- the EC is inside every building --- and that access can be monetized through post-construction services: fire alarm inspection, security monitoring, building automation maintenance. This is the vertical integration thesis, and it is the direct threat to IPS.

### Capital Sources

The capital flowing into EC rollups comes from the full spectrum of PE investment:

- **Large-cap PE firms** (Apollo, KKR, Blackstone) are deploying billion-dollar platforms
- **Mid-market PE firms** (26North Partners --- $32B AUM, Joshua Harris/Apollo co-founder; Leonard Green; Court Square) are building $500M-$2B platforms
- **Infrastructure and energy-transition funds** are entering EC as a play on electrification
- **Family offices and sovereign wealth funds** are co-investing alongside PE sponsors

The fire/security sector saw sovereign wealth fund entry in January 2025 (ADIA and GIC investing in Pye-Barker at $6.1 billion). The EC sector is attracting comparable institutional capital, validating the thesis at the highest levels of global capital allocation.

## The Vertical Integration Thesis: Own the Door, Own the RMR

### The Economic Logic

The vertical integration of electrical contractors into fire alarm and security services follows a clear economic logic built on three pillars:

**Pillar 1: The EC is already inside the building.**

Electrical contractors are the first trade on a commercial construction site (after concrete and steel) and the last to leave. They wire every circuit, every panel, every conduit. They are present during the phase when fire alarm and security system rough-in occurs. In many cases, the EC already pulls the low-voltage cable that fire alarm and security systems run on. The marginal cost of adding fire/security installation to an EC's scope of work is lower than the cost for a standalone integrator to mobilize separately --- the EC's crew is already on site, already badged, already coordinated with the GC.

For IPS, this is the critical competitive reality: 43% of IPS's revenue flows through EC subcontractor relationships. IPS does not control the customer relationship on these projects --- the EC does. The EC holds the GC contract. IPS is the sub. If the EC develops internal fire/security capability, IPS's role is eliminated entirely.

**Pillar 2: Subcontractor margin capture.**

When an EC subcontracts fire alarm work to IPS, the EC typically marks up IPS's price by 10-15% before passing the cost to the general contractor. This markup represents revenue and margin that the EC does not earn on the actual work --- it is a coordination fee. If the EC brings fire/security capability in-house, it captures both the coordination margin and the installation margin. For a $500 million EC spending $20 million annually on fire/security subcontractors, bringing that work in-house at even a 5% margin improvement represents $1 million in incremental EBITDA --- and it eliminates the risk of subcontractor schedule disruption.

**Pillar 3: Post-construction RMR.**

The construction project ends, but the building remains. Fire alarm systems require annual inspection and testing under NFPA 72. Security systems require monitoring. Access control systems require credential management. The EC that installed the system (or managed the installation as prime contractor) has the customer relationship, the system documentation, and the building access to convert the construction project into a recurring revenue stream. This is the prize PE investors seek: predictable, contract-based, inflation-protected cash flow that persists for the life of the building.

The three pillars combine into a flywheel: the EC wins the construction contract (access) -> installs fire/security as part of scope (margin capture) -> converts the installed base to inspection, monitoring, and managed services contracts (RMR). Each building becomes a long-term revenue stream rather than a one-time project.

## The Displacement Mechanism

Understanding how EC vertical integration threatens IPS requires mapping the specific mechanisms through which displacement occurs. There are four primary vectors:

### 1. Scope Absorption

The most direct displacement mechanism. When an EC develops internal fire/security capability, it absorbs the scope of work that would previously have been subcontracted to IPS. On a $10 million commercial construction project, fire alarm might represent $300,000-$800,000 and security another $200,000-$500,000. If the EC performs this work with its own crews, IPS never sees the bid.

**Current state:** IPS receives fire alarm and security scope from ECs as a named subcontractor on the GC's bid. The EC includes IPS's price in its proposal, adds markup, and manages the subcontractor relationship.

**Future state:** The EC's internal fire/security division bids the scope directly. IPS is not invited. The EC captures both the installation margin and the markup that previously compensated it for managing IPS.

**Speed of displacement:** Moderate. Building internal fire/security capability requires hiring NICET-certified technicians, obtaining fire alarm contractor licenses, and establishing manufacturer authorization (Edwards, Notifier, etc.). This takes 18-36 months for a well-capitalized EC platform.

### 2. Vendor List Consolidation

PE-backed EC platforms standardize their subcontractor and vendor lists across all operating companies. When a PE firm acquires multiple ECs, the platform's procurement team evaluates which subcontractors to keep and which to consolidate. IPS may be on one EC's preferred list but not on the platform's national list. As the platform standardizes, IPS's customer relationship with the local EC subsidiary can be overridden by a platform-level decision to use a national fire/security provider (Convergint, Pye-Barker) that offers volume pricing.

**Current state:** IPS maintains relationships with individual EC companies and their local project managers.

**Future state:** A PE-backed EC platform standardizes on a national fire/security provider. Local EC subsidiaries are directed to use the platform's preferred vendor, regardless of local relationships.

**Speed of displacement:** Fast. Vendor list consolidation is one of the first operational changes PE owners implement post-acquisition, typically within 6-12 months.

### 3. Bundle Pricing

EC platforms with fire/security capability offer general contractors a bundled electrical-plus-fire-plus-security package at a lower total cost than the sum of separate subcontractor bids. The GC receives one subcontract instead of three, reducing coordination overhead, schedule risk, and administrative burden. Even if IPS's fire alarm price is competitive on a standalone basis, the bundled package from the EC may be lower in total --- and simpler to manage.

**Current state:** GCs bid electrical, fire alarm, and security as separate scopes to separate subcontractors.

**Future state:** EC platforms offer a "turnkey low-voltage package" that includes electrical, fire alarm, security, and data/communications as a single scope. GCs prefer the simplicity and cost savings.

**Speed of displacement:** Moderate to fast. Bundle pricing can be offered as soon as the EC has basic fire/security capability, even if the quality lags IPS's.

### 4. Post-Construction Capture

The EC that installs the fire alarm and security system has the as-built documentation, the customer relationship, and the building access. It is the natural incumbent for the post-construction service contract: annual fire alarm inspection, security system monitoring, access control credential management. If the EC offers these services, the building owner sees no reason to engage a separate integrator. The recurring revenue that would have flowed to IPS --- the inspection contract, the monitoring contract, the managed services agreement --- flows instead to the EC.

**Current state:** IPS installs systems as a sub to the EC on new construction, then pursues the owner directly for post-construction service contracts. This requires IPS to identify the building owner, make contact, and sell the service relationship --- a step that many integrators skip (the "install-train-walk-away" model).

**Future state:** The EC's fire/security division installs the system and transitions the customer to its own service department. IPS never has the opportunity to compete for the post-construction revenue.

**Speed of displacement:** Slow to moderate. Post-construction capture requires the EC to build a service infrastructure (dispatch, technicians, monitoring). But it is the most valuable displacement vector because it captures RMR --- the highest-margin, longest-duration revenue stream.

A dividend recap of this magnitude signals total confidence in the platform's cash flows. Critically, the "recurring revenue loan" flag on ArchKey's $650 million term loan B means **lenders are underwriting based on recurring revenue from fire and security services**, not just project revenue. The credit markets are already pricing ArchKey as a fire/security platform, not just an electrical contractor.

And the PE firm behind it has $32 billion in AUM, $3.85 billion in dry powder, and a co-founder who built Apollo Global Management. When ArchKey's fire/security team reaches full operational capacity, the remaining $130,000 will go to zero. PEC Solutions is the canary in the coal mine for the entire EC customer portfolio --- and the canary is backed by one of the most sophisticated capital allocators in private equity.

## Implications for Independent Integrators

The convergence of two consolidation waves --- PE rollup of fire/security integrators and PE rollup of electrical contractors with vertical integration into fire/security --- creates a structural threat to independent integrators that is qualitatively different from traditional competitive pressure.

### The Convergence Loop

The two consolidation waves are not parallel --- they are converging. The loop operates as follows:

1. **PE buys EC** --- the middle of the value chain (construction and installation)
2. **EC gets GC relationships and job site access** --- the EC is already inside every building, already holds the subcontractor relationships with general contractors
3. **EC builds or acquires fire/security capability** --- the top of the value chain (design, engineering, specialized installation)
4. **EC captures subcontractor margin AND recurring revenue tail** --- the installation margin that was flowing to independent integrators like IPS, plus the post-construction inspection, monitoring, and managed services contracts
5. **Independent integrators lose the channel** --- the door to the building is now owned by the company that used to hold it open

The mechanism is straightforward: PE buys EC -> EC gets GC relationships and job site access -> EC builds or acquires fire/security capability -> EC captures subcontractor margin AND recurring revenue tail -> independent integrators like IPS lose the channel. Each step follows logically from the last. Each step is already observable in IPS's own customer data.

This is not hypothetical. ArchKey (IPS's customer, backed by 26North Partners with $32B AUM and $3.85B in dry powder, led by Apollo co-founder Joshua Harris) is executing this playbook right now --- 47x valuation growth in 7 years, a $475M dividend recap in March 2026, and lenders underwriting the $650M TLB on recurring fire/security revenue --- and PEC Solutions' revenue to IPS has already dropped 89% as a result. APi Group executed it with the $3.1 billion Chubb acquisition, transforming from a $2 billion EC into the world's largest life safety provider ($7.911B revenue, $21.25B EV, 16.80x EV/EBITDA --- with subsidiaries All State Fire and Security in Harrison, OH and Mid-Ohio Pipeline Services in Mansfield, OH). EMCOR operates hundreds of NICET-certified experts through its CRT subsidiary. IES Holdings is growing its Communications segment at 56% annually. Apollo's State Group is assembling a fire, security, and communications platform across Kentucky, Indiana, and Tennessee --- IPS's exact geography. And The Superior Group, IPS's single largest EC customer at $8.8 million in three-year revenue, is expanding into security and access controls.

### What This Means for IPS Specifically

**1. The subcontractor model is structurally vulnerable.** IPS generates 43% of its revenue through EC subcontractor relationships. This model works when ECs do not have fire/security capability and need a specialist subcontractor. As ECs develop or acquire this capability --- whether through PE-driven vertical integration or organic expansion --- the subcontractor model breaks down. IPS cannot rely on EC partners whose long-term incentive is to eliminate the subcontractor.

**2. The displacement is already underway --- and now empirically confirmed in IPS's own data.** This is not a 10-year warning. PEC Solutions revenue to IPS dropped 89% in a single period --- from $1.2 million to $130,000 --- as ArchKey's internal fire/security capability came online. That is not a forecast; it is a measurement. The PE firm behind ArchKey has $32 billion in AUM and $3.85 billion in undeployed capital. It just extracted $475 million in a dividend recap --- one month ago --- signaling that the recurring fire/security revenue base is strong enough to support aggressive leverage. ArchKey is hiring NICET estimators today. EMCOR has hundreds of NICET-certified experts through its CRT subsidiary today. IES Holdings grew its fire/security segment 56% last year. The State Group is acquiring fire/security companies in IPS's geography today. The displacement mechanism is not approaching --- it has arrived.

**3. Direct customer relationships are the only durable defense.** When IPS is a subcontractor to an EC, the building owner may not even know IPS exists. The EC holds the contract. The EC holds the relationship. If IPS transitions to displacement, it loses both the project revenue and the post-construction service opportunity. The only way to make IPS's customer relationships displacement-proof is to own them directly --- to be the fire/security provider that the building owner contracts with, not the subcontractor that the EC manages.

**4. RMR acceleration becomes even more urgent.** The vertical integration thesis is fundamentally about RMR. PE investors value ECs at 5-6x because construction revenue is project-based and cyclical. They value fire/security integrators at 10-14x because RMR is predictable and durable. ECs are adding fire/security capability specifically to access RMR. If IPS does not accelerate its own RMR conversion --- from the current 7-10% to the 25%+ industry benchmark --- it will be outcompeted for post-construction service contracts by EC platforms that are investing hundreds of millions to build exactly this capability.

**5. The window is shorter than previously estimated.** The fire/security consolidation threat (Convergint, Pye-Barker) was identified as a 3-5 year strategic challenge. The EC vertical integration threat compresses this timeline. EC platforms do not need to build fire/security capability from scratch --- they can acquire it in 90 days, the same way they acquired their electrical businesses. Apollo's State Group acquired three companies with fire/security capability in IPS's geography within a single year. The window for IPS to transition from EC-dependent to EC-independent is 2-3 years, not 5.

**6. Tennessee is the best near-term opportunity --- and the most urgent defensive priority.** Among IPS's three-state footprint, Tennessee has the least PE-backed competitor presence in fire/security. But Zeus Fire and Security (Access Holdings) has entered Tennessee, and the State Group (Apollo) is acquiring across the KY/IN/TN corridor. IPS's Nashville branch, led by locally-hired Charles Robinson, is the fastest-growing operation. Accelerating Nashville's growth and establishing IPS as the dominant independent fire/security integrator in Middle Tennessee before PE-backed EC platforms arrive is both the highest-return growth opportunity and the most urgent defensive action.

### Strategic Response Framework

IPS's response to the EC vertical integration threat should operate on three timelines:

**Immediate (0-12 months):**
- Map every EC customer's ownership status and fire/security capability quarterly
- Identify building owners behind EC subcontractor projects and begin direct relationship development
- Accelerate post-construction service contract capture on every project installed through the EC channel
- Monitor ArchKey/PEC Solutions job postings and capability announcements

**Near-term (12-36 months):**
- Shift revenue mix from 43% EC-dependent to 30% or below through direct customer acquisition
- Accelerate Nashville growth to establish market position before PE-backed EC platforms arrive
- Develop data center fire/security capability to capture the Ohio pipeline directly
- Build relationships with GCs independent of the EC channel

**Medium-term (3-5 years):**
- Achieve 25%+ RMR to make IPS's revenue profile resilient to EC channel disruption
- Establish direct relationships with the top 50 building owners in each IPS market
- Position IPS as the independent alternative to PE-consolidated EC/fire/security platforms
- Evaluate whether any independent EC partners are candidates for strategic alliance or cross-referral agreements that formalize the relationship against platform-level disruption
